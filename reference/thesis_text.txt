Introduction
Voice is the foremost vehicle of thought: a vivid and living bridge between the soul and the world beyond. Through voice we articulate our thoughts, respond to others, engage in dialogue and song, and give expression to the full range of human emotion. For many, voice is more than expression-it is their livelihood: the teacher, the singer, the orator, and even the physician all depend on the clarity, strength, and control of their voice for the practice of their art. This was no less true in antiquity: among Greeks and Romans alike, the voice was indispensable to public life, from the deliberations of the orator and the instructions of the teacher to songs of the chorus and the diagnoses of the physician. As Aristotle declares: 

…σημαντικὸς γὰρ δή τις ψόφος ἐστὶν ἡ φωνή.
…Voice is indeed a kind of a meaningful sound.
Arist. De Anima. 420b

According to Aristotle, the voice stands apart from a mere sound. The voice is a vehicle of signification, bound with reason and expression. Therefore, it is fundamental to human communication and society. No one articulated the centrality of the voice more powerfully than Galen, who declared that “The voice, which reports the thoughts of the mind, is the most important of all the works of the soul…” Yet despite this striking prominence that Galen assigns to the voice, the physiological processes underlying its production remain, to this day, scattered across his vast corpus and are often overlooked in modern scholarship. Galen wrote a treatise dedicated to voice production: Περὶ Φωνής (On the Voice). He frequently refers to it in his other works when discussing a stage of voice production or one of the parts involved. Only a later Latin adaptation, filtered through an Arabic intermediary, remains, and its fragmentary state, both conceptually and materially, renders it an insufficient basis for reconstructing his doctrine on its own. This version will serve as “supportive” testimony throughout this essay, reinforcing and substantiating the analysis of the ideas in the original surviving material. Galen’s own voice was metaphorically taken from him by the loss of his treatise on voice, prompting us to examine the echoes of this treatise scattered throughout the rest of the collection. Enough material survives across Galen’s extant treatises to allow for a systematic, if complex, reconstruction of his theory of voice production. Rather than offering a single, unified account, Galen disperses his observations across anatomical, physiological, and philosophical oeuvres, each illuminating different facets of the process.
The difficulty, however, extends beyond the loss of a single treatise. The material necessary for reconstructing Galen’s theory of phonation is dispersed across a wide range of his writings: anatomical treatises such as his major anatomical work De Anatomicis Administrationibus (AA), his major physiological work, De usu partium (UP), argumentative and polemical texts such as De Placitis Hippocratis et Platonis (PHP), other treatises such as De motu musculorum and De locis affectis, and the aforementioned Latin De Voce. None of these works contains a complete or self-sufficient account of voice production. Instead, each contributes isolated or, at best, thematically adjacent observations that illuminate aspects of the process: the topography of laryngeal cartilages, the muscular connections in the laryngeal region, the neural control of phonatory movements, the nature of ἐκφύσησις (forceful exhalation), or the role of the glōttis in modulating air. These observations were never assembled into a surviving unified discussion. As a result, the modern interpreter is confronted with a considerable methodological challenge: how to reconstruct a coherent physiological model from materials that were neither arranged nor composed with such unity in mind. This challenge is sharpened by the state of preservation of Galen’s main anatomical account: the later books of AA, where the most detailed anatomical description of the organs of phonation is found, survive only in the medieval Arabic translation of Ḥubaiš. The Greek text is lost. While the Arabic text is rich, precise, and in many cases indispensable, it nevertheless demands careful philological scrutiny, including the triangulation of Arabic anatomical vocabulary with the terminology of Galen’s earlier Greek books and the cross-referencing provided by Simon’s translation and lexicon. The Arabic text, moreover, reflects a practical, didactic anatomical programme tailored to its linguistic and cultural context. The reconstruction of the original Greek concepts thus requires interpretive caution. Compounding the problem, descriptions of particular structures, such as the sternothyroid muscles, the omohyoid muscles, or the recurrent laryngeal nerves, appear in multiple works, and the accounts sometimes diverge, whether through topical focus or differences arising from Galen’s own dissection of different animal species (notably the ape and the pig). Such divergences are not merely terminological; they have direct implications for how the anatomy of phonation, as Galen conceived it, is to be understood.
Despite the centrality of voice in Galen’s thought and the richness of the material dispersed throughout his corpus, modern scholarship has yet to produce a comprehensive study that reconstructs Galen’s understanding of phonation as a unified physiological process. Existing studies tend to approach the subject tangentially—often as part of broader discussions on respiration, neurology, or the functions of the psychē. For instance, Peter N. Singer’s analysis of Galen’s pneuma theory dives into the roles of vital and psychic pneuma in physiology and psychology. Still, it does not focus specifically on the mechanisms of voice production. Similarly, while Heinrich von Staden has illuminated aspects of Galen’s anatomical experiments, and R. J. Hankinson has explored the interplay between body and spirit in Greek medicine, their works do not synthesise Galen’s dispersed observations into a coherent theory of voice production. Likewise, while Armelle Debru has provided valuable discussions of the trachea and the respiratory system in her extensive account of respiration in Galen, these do not address the specific manner in which the larynx transforms ἐκφύσησις into voice. Indeed, the secondary literature has primarily concentrated on isolated elements: the role of the lungs and thorax in respiration, the conceptual place of pneuma in Galenic psychology, or the structure and function of specific nerves and muscles. While these studies offer valuable insights, they do not attempt to synthesise Galen’s dispersed observations into a coherent theory of voice production. More importantly, there has been no dedicated effort to map out the anatomical parts involved in phonation, such as the glottis, the laryngeal muscles, the laryngeal nerves, and other supportive body parts in a way that reflects Galen’s own integrated anatomical and physiological framework. This lacuna is particularly surprising given Galen’s explicit recognition of voice as the most important work of the soul, and the complexity with which he describes its corporeal foundations. The absence of a systematic study not only hinders our understanding of a significant portion of Galen’s medical thought but also obscures the ways in which he linked physical structure to psychological and rhetorical concerns.
This study aims to address this lacuna by reconstructing and analysing Galen's theory of phonation. This topic has been surprisingly underexamined despite the central role Galen himself assigns to the voice. The central question guiding this inquiry is: How does Galen conceive the anatomical structures involved in phonation, and how does he understand their coordinated action in transforming ἐκφύσησις (forceful exhalation) into voice? A second, closely related question concerns the intellectual architecture that underpins Galen’s explanation: How does his teleological reasoning, his conviction that every part of the body is crafted with skill (τέχνη) and forethought (πρόνοια), serve as the epistemic principle that binds his anatomical descriptions and physiological analyses into a coherent, intelligible system? From these questions emerges the principal argument of this thesis: Galen’s accounts of the anatomy and physiology of voice production, although scattered across several works, form an integrated and internally coherent model whose unity becomes apparent only when the material is examined in its totality. This model is fundamentally teleological.  Galen does not merely describe the organs involved in phonation; he explains why each part possesses the material composition, spatial arrangement, and mechanical properties it does, and how these features allow it to fulfil its function within a larger system. The cartilaginous structure of the trachea, the laryngeal cartilages, the muscular configuration of the larynx, the bilateral organisation of the phonatory apparatus, and the unique nature of the glōttis are all, in his account, manifestations of nature’s purposive design. Teleology thus provides the intelligible order through which the physiological mechanism of ἐκφύσησις, traveling in the trachea, modified in the glōttis, struck by the cartilages of the larynx, and resonated through the airway, can be explained. 
While this work has broader implications for understanding Galen’s intellectual method, it focuses specifically on the anatomy and physiological mechanisms of voice production in his corpus, drawing primarily from his anatomical and physiological works. Its aim is not to offer a philosophical or cultural interpretation of the concept of voice in antiquity, nor to trace the broader rhetorical, psychological, or metaphysical dimensions of phōnē in Galen’s thought. Such inquiries, while important, fall outside the scope of this study due to their breadth. Moreover, this study is focused solely on voice, not on what happens after it emerges: once it appears, it can be shaped into articulations and speech. This shaping involves body parts not essential to voice emission (e.g., the uvula, the tongue, and the teeth), as well as broader psychological dimensions in the study of voice. Similarly, it will not cover modulation of voice in terms of pitch and timbre.
Instead, this study is grounded in Galen’s physiological and anatomical descriptions to reconstruct how he understood the body’s generation of voice as a corporeal process, coordinated by breath, muscles, nerves, and specific anatomical structures. By narrowing the inquiry in this way, this work aims to clarify Galen’s explanatory model of phonation and assess its internal coherence within the broader framework of his medical theory, demonstrating that it aligns well with his teleological thinking. By tracing Galen’s discussion of the structures and functions involved in vocal production, we may also come to see more clearly how one of antiquity’s most outstanding physicians understood the development of knowledge itself—and how anatomical insight transformed physiological doctrine. It offers a test case for Galen’s anatomical epistemology: how he builds his physiological models from anatomical observation. In doing so, it contributes to our understanding not only of ancient phonation theory but also of Galen’s explanatory style, his use of analogy, and his relationship to both philosophical thought and empirical dissection. However, such physiological inquiry requires a strong anatomical basis: one cannot understand the coordinated function of body organs without understanding the nature of those organs. As will be demonstrated, such anatomical inquiry is essential, as Galen’s conception of several bodily structures differs substantially from the modern understanding and warrants independent study. 
The reconstruction offered in this thesis relies on a close philological and anatomical method, and on a careful delimitation of the scope of inquiry. The primary sources for the anatomical analysis are the seventh book of UP and the eleventh and fourteenth books of AA, the latter two surviving only in Arabic. Supplementary anatomical details are found in De dissectione musculorum and other, less comprehensive treatises. The physiological analysis draws chiefly on UP 7.13, PHP 2, and the Latin De Voce, alongside relevant passages from De locis affectis, De foetuum formatione, and other works in which Galen discusses exhalation, muscular motion, and the mechanics of sound. Throughout, special attention is paid to Galen’s conceptual vocabulary: terms such as τι σῶμα (a certain body), ὕλη οἰκεία (proper material), ἐκφύσησις (forceful exhalation), πληγή (strike), ῥώμη (force), ῥυθμισθῆναι (to shape), and to the analogies he employs to guide the reader’s comprehension, particularly those drawn from music (with an emphasis on the analogy between the glōttis and the mouthpiece of the aulos). The methodological aim is not to impose external models, but to reconstruct Galen’s explanations on their own terms, using the conceptual resources internal to his writings.
This work proceeds in two chapters. The first chapter reconstructs the anatomy of the phonatory system: the fixed group of body parts described by Galen as working together in the sake of phonation. The chapter begins with an introduction that provides historical background to the anatomical inquiries into voice and positions Galen as a central figure and his work as an unprecedented foundation for the study of the anatomy of phonation. The analysis commences with the structures that indirectly support phonation: the lungs, the thoracic and abdominal musculature, and the trachea. The study focuses primarily on the structures that constitute the framework, power, and operative components of the laryngeal mechanism. I am suggesting dividing the relevant body parts into five categories, in a combination of Galen’s categorisation of muscles with contemporary interpretation and inclusion of the other body parts: 1. Framework, 2. Power and support structures (including the eight other muscles), 3. The Special Muscles (intrinsic muscles of the larynx), 4. Laryngeal nerves, 5. Laryngeal framework (the components of the larynx). Particular emphasis is placed on the glōttis, belonging to the laryngeal framework. The glōttis is described in UP.7.13 as a unique structure with no similar counterpart in the whole body, as well as ‘the first and most proper organ of the voice.’ Therefore, it is central to understanding the entire system. Throughout the chapter, the anatomical descriptions are analysed in light of Galen’s teleological reasoning: the material composition, spatial arrangement, and functional integration of each organ are shown to reflect nature’s purposive design. The second chapter examines the physiological mechanisms that act upon this anatomical foundation to produce voice. The chapter begins by analysing the concept of ἐκφύσησις, which Galen defines as “a kind of suitable material of voice” (ὕλη τις οἰκεία φωνῆς). This ἐκφύσησις, which is forceful exhalation, is distinguished from regular exhalation (ἐκπνοή). Through a close reading of several passages in the Galenic corpus, the chapter reconstructs Galen’s understanding of ἐκφύσησις as a forceful, muscularly generated exhalation with degrees of intensity, and as an activity (ἐνέργεια) rather than a passive movement. The discussion then turns to the laryngeal mechanism and the modification of ἐκφύσησις in it: the narrowing of the glōttis, the role of the cartilages in striking the accelerated ἐκφύσησις, the contribution of the special muscles and their innervation by the laryngeal nerves, and the essential interplay and proportionality (συμμετρία) between air and cartilages. The glōttis will be once again a central focus, as it is the very place in which ἐκφύσησις undergoes the necessary modification. Through an integrative analysis of UP 7.13, PHP 2, and On the Voice, the chapter argues that voice arises only when the force of ἐκφύσησις is strong enough to exert a significant impact on the laryngeal cartilages, a state achieved only with the narrowing of the glōttis. The chapter concludes by placing Galen’s physiological account within his teleological framework, showing that the laryngeal mechanism exemplifies the purposeful arrangement of parts that characterise his entire anatomical and physiological method. 
By examining phonation as a test case for Galenic explanation, the study illuminates his broader anatomical epistemology: the integration of observation, conceptual analysis, and teleological reasoning that underlies his accounts of bodily function. In doing so, it provides the first comprehensive reconstruction of Galen’s doctrine of phonation and offers a new perspective on the development of ancient anatomical and physiological thought.

Chapter 1 – Anatomy 
Introduction
This chapter aims to assemble and elucidate descriptions of the group of organs involved in the physiological process of voice production as presented in Galen’s works. The analysis begins with auxiliary supportive structures that provide air to the phonatory system, proceeds through the organs that set the framework and boundaries of the system, and culminates at the larynx, the centre of the phonatory system, where voice emerges.  Galen’s accounts of voice production include numerous descriptions of the body parts involved. Reaching such a goal is not straightforward; it involves several challenges. First, the descriptions are scattered across the Galenic corpus. Galen’s main account on the production of voice, On the Voice, is lost. The primary account on the organs of voice production comes from On Anatomical Procedures. In his eleventh book of On Anatomical Procedures, surviving only in the Arabic translation of Ḥubaiš, Galen thoroughly describes the anatomy of the organs of phonation. Galen leads the reader through a comprehensive dissection of a rhesus macaque (Lynx Monkey) and a vivisection of a pig in the fourteenth book, explaining the anatomical features of the dissected parts in step-by-step detail. Galen describes their topographical qualities: their arrangement, the relationship between their location and that of other parts, etc. Moreover, their composition: size, hardness, the materials used in their composition, etc. Another significant account comes from the seventh book of “On the Usefulness of Parts” (UP). The seventh book is dedicated to the organs of respiration and voice production. Additional details on the muscles of voice production can be found in On Dissection of Muscles, chapters 12-17. Second, because some organs are described in more than one treatise, the descriptions do not always overlap; rather, a second description adds details absent from the first. For example, the description of the sternohyoid muscles in AA is rather superficial, while Musc. Diss. adds essential details on their composition and topography. 
Another notable example is the description of the sternothyroid muscles. In UP, Galen says that there could be two of these in small animals and four in larger ones. In AA, it is explicitly noted that there are two muscles. Third, as stated above, the original Greek version of the later books of AA did not survive. In my work, I will consult the original Arabic text while integrating Simon’s and Duckworth’s interpretations. The need for the Arabic text will arise especially in clarifying terminology and identifying organs. Simon’s lexicon will facilitate cross-referencing Arabic and Greek terms as they appear in the earlier books of AA and other Galenic works.  Therefore, the task of collecting the pieces is not as straightforward as a close reading of one continuous account. 
Although Galen’s account of voice production is the most comprehensive, he was not the first to study voice. As befits an essential human characteristic, this has been a focus of interest since the dawn of medicine. The medical interest in voice production was first sparked by concern with vocal impairments, which is already tangentially attested in epic literature. A passage from Homer, discussed in Plutarch’s Quaestiones Convivales, indicates a recognition of the trachea as the passage of air, affecting voice: 
Τοιαῦτα τοῦ Νικίου διεξιόντος ὁ γραμματικὸς Πρωτογένης ἔφη συνεωρακέναι πρῶτον Ὅμηρον, ὅτι τῆς μὲν τροφῆς ὁ στόμαχος ἀγγεῖόν ἐστιν, τοῦ δὲ πνεύματος ὁ βρόγχος, ὃν ἀσφάραγον ἐκάλουν οἱ Eπαλαιοί· διὸ καὶ τοὺς μεγαλοφώνους “ἐρισφαράγους” ἐπονομάζειν εἰώθασιν· εἰπὼν οὖν ὅτι τοῦ Ἕκτορος ὁ Ἀχιλλεὺς ἤλασε
	λευκανίην, ἵνα τε ψυχῆς ὤκιστος ὄλεθρος·
οὐδ᾿ ἄρ᾿ ἀπ᾿ ἀσφάραγον μελίη τάμε χαλκοβάρεια,
ὄφρα τί μιν προτιείποι ἀμειβόμενος ἐπέεσσιν,
τὸν ἀσφάραγον ὄντα φωνῆς ἴδιον ὀχετὸν καὶ πνεύματος, τὴν δὲ λευκανίην τροφῆς ἀγγεῖον λέγει ἐν τούτοις·
	νῦν δὴ καὶ σίτου πασάμην καὶ αἴθοπα οἶνον
λευκανιὴν ἐσέθηκα.
After this exposition by Nicias, the schoolmaster Protogenes remarked that Homer was the first to have observed that the receptacle for our nourishment is the oesophagus, and for breath the windpipe, which the ancients called aspharagus (so that the epithet for loud-voiced persons was erispkaragoi). When Homer says that Achilles struck Hector’s “throat (leukanië), where life is quickest to perish, but the bronze-heavy ashen spear cut not his windpipe (aspkaragos), that he might speak and exchange words with him,” he is speaking of the windpipe (aspharagos) as the special channel of voice and breath, but he speaks of the throat (leuhanië) as the receptacle of nourishment in the following:
Now I have tasted food and poured bright wine down my throat. 
Plut. Mor. Quaest. Conv. 7. A.2, 213,4-15 Hubert = 498D Stephanus (trans. P. A. Clement and H. B. Hoffleit)
This passage suggests that already in Homer we find a rudimentary functional anatomy relevant to voice production. By distinguishing the oesophagus as the channel of nourishment and the trachea (ἀσφάραγος) as the channel of breath, the text distinguishes the passage of breath in a specific place in the throat as an undifferentiated region. The Homeric example of Hector, who can still speak after his throat is wounded because the windpipe itself is not cut, reinforces this distinction and emphasises voice production: life and nourishment are threatened, but phonation remains possible. Although far from a physiological theory, this differentiation anticipates later medical reflection by presupposing that voice depends on the integrity of a specific bodily channel associated with breath, a conceptual move that would be developed and systematised in later anatomical and physiological accounts. 
Early medical testimonies, notably from the Hippocratic corpus, mention cases of impaired voice, often characterised by hoarseness (βράγχος, βραγχώδης). The terms typically pertain to the chest and throat, indicating a deeper understanding of the anatomical structures involved in voice production. Moreover, Hippocratic physicians used the different qualities of voice as a paradigm to assess their patients' condition. Vocal characteristics such as “clarity and roughness (φωνῆς λαμπρότης καὶ τρηχύτης) were tracked by the physician and helped him recognise diseases which might not have been manifested with visible symptoms.
The next landmark of voice studies in antiquity is Aristotle. Yet, treating voice primarily in philosophical and zoological contexts, Aristotle did not write any comprehensive analysis of the anatomy of voice production. Aristotle was aware of organs like the trachea and the larynx, as well as some of their properties, such as the cartilaginous structure of the trachea (and possibly the larynx as well). Nevertheless, he did not outline a thorough anatomical examination of the structures responsible for the production of voice. Moreover, the terms larynx, pharynx, and artēria are used interchangeably, and often describe the windpipe or throat in general. The study of voice extended from Aristotle to Hellenistic times, with the philosophical context of Aristotle’s inquiries (rather than the anatomical) echoing in the Stoic tradition. Thus, they were focused on the difference between voice and speech. Building on Aristotle’s distinction between voice and speech, they introduced an intermediate stage, dividing the process into three levels: voice (φωνή), articulate voice (λέξις), and meaningful articulate voice (λόγος). The production of these three sounds is treated by them mainly from a philosophical perspective.
Turning next to the fragments of Herophilus, we find that the study of voice is not explicitly attested. Nevertheless, several fragments (preserved through Galen, Rufus, and Pollux) indicate that Herophilus investigated the anatomical structures of the throat/connection to the throat. Moreover, Herophilus, who anatomically isolated the nervous system and identified its physiological functions in voluntary motion, accorded pneuma a central role. It is therefore reasonable to presume that he may also have discussed the physiology of the parts of phonation, in which pneuma plays a key role, in works that have since been lost. Another notable mention in the study of voice comes from “Anonymous Parisinus”. In his work, dated to the first century CE, the Anonymous surveys different types of diseases, some of which feature impairments of the voice: loss of voice (ἀφωνία), voice without tone (φωνὴ ἄτονος), and hoarse voice (τραχυνθείη ἡ φωνὴ). The subsequent development in the anatomical and physiological studies of voice production comes in the same century from Rufus of Ephesus, who challenged a long-held belief about blood vessels in voice production: 
Καρωτίδας δὲ τὰς διὰ τοῦ τραχήλου κοίλας ὠνόμαζον πάλαι, ὅτιπιεζόντων καρώδεις καὶ ἄφωνοι ἐγίνοντο· ὤφθη δὲ νῦν τὸ πάθημαοὐ τῶν ἀρτηριῶν, ἀλλὰ νεύρων αἰσθητικῶν πεφυκότων πλησίον· 
The ancients called the arteries through the neck ‘carotids’, since when they were compressed, people became drowsy (karōs-like) and voiceless (aphōnoi). However, it has now been observed that this condition does not stem from the arteries, but from the nerves that are nearby. 
Rufus, De Corporis Humani Appellationibus. 210, 163,9-11 Daremberg
Rufus provides the first evidence on the specific connection between phonation and the nervous system, linking nerves to the organs of voice production. Nevertheless, Galen is our earliest surviving evidence for comprehensive studies of the parts involved in phonation. His investigations went far beyond previous studies, providing detailed examinations of the entire vocal apparatus through careful dissection and observation. While the first extended discussion on the nature and function of nerves in the larynx area is reserved for Rufus, Galen was the first to discover and name the critical recurrent laryngeal nerve, which comes down from the brain and branches in the larynx area and controls almost all of the muscles related to the larynx. While prominent in phonation, the recurrent laryngeal nerve is one of many body parts participating in phonation that are described in the detailed anatomical works of Galen.
Considering the extensive, complex, and comprehensive background of voice production studies, Galen’s anatomical work represents a turning point in the study of voice. Earlier traditions (medical, philosophical, and anatomical) had identified specific organs and speculated about their roles, yet none had assembled them into a coherent system grounded in the methodology of dissection. Galen’s achievement lies precisely in transforming scattered observations into an integrated account of phonation, in which each structure is described according to its form, position, and material composition, and related teleologically to its function. The following sections of this chapter will therefore reconstruct this system from across Galen’s corpus, synthesising the dispersed testimonies to reveal how the organs of respiration and phonation function in coordination within a physiological framework.

Before discussing the parts in detail, one recurring feature of Galen’s anatomical descriptions deserves particular attention: his consistent emphasis on paired structures and their bilateral arrangement, a pattern that warrants closer examination.
Paired Organs in the Galenic Ponatory System
Galen's detailed anatomical descriptions of the phonatory system reveal a deep comprehension of bilateral symmetry as a fundamental organising principle in human anatomy. The concept of anatomical symmetry already occupies a central place in Aristotle’s biological thought, ranking immediately after teleology in importance. The body is divided into three axes, forming three pairs: above/below, front/back, and right/left, which are referred to as the dimensions of magnitude. Galen theorises about anatomical symmetry as a concept, following the Canon, a now lost treatise of bodily proportions, of the classical sculptor and mathematician Polykleitos; his careful documentation of paired structures and their relationships demonstrates an implicit recognition of this anatomical pattern. The phonatory system, as described by Galen, exhibits numerous examples of bilateral symmetry, particularly in its muscular and nervous components. Throughout the anatomical overview, we can observe the mentions of paired muscles, nerves, and three other paired structures. This symmetrical arrangement is not merely an observational detail but appears to be integral to Galen's understanding of normal anatomical organisation. His description of paired muscles, such as the thyroarytenoid and cricoarytenoid muscles (which affect the movements of the glottis), consistently acknowledges their bilateral nature, suggesting that he viewed this symmetry as fundamentally important to their function. 
Particularly noteworthy is Galen's treatment of the nervous system components: His discovery and description of the recurrent laryngeal nerves as "two small nerves ascending from below" emphasises their paired nature. This understanding of nervous system symmetry represents a significant advancement in anatomical knowledge, as it relates directly to understanding voice production and its impairment. Galen's consistent attention to bilateral symmetry throughout his anatomical descriptions, particularly in the muscular and nervous components of voice production, reveals that he conceptualised the phonatory apparatus not as a random collection of parts, but as an organised system following predictable anatomical principles. With this understanding of Galen's recognition of bilateral symmetry as an organising principle, it behoves us to proceed to a systematic examination of the phonatory system's structures.
Auxiliary Supportive structures 
Galen refers to the lungs as “an organ both of respiration and voice” because they receive air and provide it to the phonatory system, which transforms it into voice. The thoracic & abdominal muscles are necessary supportive structures of the phonatory system, as their contraction compresses the lungs, increasing air speed. This transformation makes the ‘regular exhalation’ (ἐκπνοή) into a ‘forceful exhalation’ (ἐκφύσησις), which makes the proper material of voice. The ἐκφύσησις reaches the larynx through the trachea. In UP, Galen introduces the trachea, “the rough artery” (ἡ τραχεῖα ἀρτηρία), or βρόγχος, as named by “some physicians.” The trachea is defined as rough (τραχύς) as opposed to the “smooth” (λεπτός) arteries that carry blood. It originates in the pharynx and descends into the lungs, where it branches and retains its characteristic structure deep within the pulmonary tissue. It is composed of cartilaginous rings that are incomplete posteriorly, with the gaps filled by membranous fibres. For Galen, the fibres are only membranous (ὑμενώδη). However, in modern anatomy, they are recognised as both membranous and muscular tissue, called the ‘trachealis muscle’. These tissues have both longitudinal and transverse fibres, which we can assume is what Galen saw. Integrating the modern anatomical knowledge with Galenic thought, we can rely on Peter Singer’s interpretation of Galen’s theory on types of fibres, and say that the trachea has longitudinal (εὐθεῖα) and transverse (ἐγκάρσια) fibres. By applying the study of Ezrokhi and Lewis on the motion of fibres in Galen to these fibres, we can say that these fibres fill the posterior gap and permit a complex range of movements, including elongation and shortening, widening and narrowing, as well as peristaltic-like contractions—the longitudinal fibres aid in drawing air inward, and the transverse fibres in expelling it.
 The cartilage rings are shaped like the letter C or lunate sigma (ϲ). It has a circular inner lining tunic that is continuous with the lining of the mouth, oesophagus, and stomach. It has an outer membrane covering the entire structure. As it enters the thorax, it divides into branches that go to the two lungs. The cartilage rings persist in the branches deep into the lung tissue. Galen emphasises that the trachea contains no blood in its normal state (despite being called an artery). As Armelle Debru has noted, this dual structure, comprising cartilage and membrane, expresses Galen’s fondness for antithetical pairs: hardness and softness, as well as mobility and immobility. Its structure allows it to lengthen and shorten, as well as to widen and narrow, during breathing. The membranous part allows the trachea not to obstruct the oesophagus when it expands while swallowing, and the cartilage provides rigidity to keep the airway open, which aids in voice production. Thus, the trachea’s morphology embodies a balance between rigidity and flexibility, ensuring the integrity and continuity of both breathing and voice production. 
While these organs are crucial to the phonatory system, without them the system would lack the material necessary to produce voice; voice is not created within those organs. Additionally, they are crucial to another bodily system: the respiratory system. For these reasons, I excluded these organs from the phonatory system. A detailed anatomical description of these organs will not be given. The terminology I will use throughout this chapter is the modern term for the body parts. I do so for two reasons: first, all the parts mentioned by Galen exist in human anatomy and are clearly defined in modern anatomical lexicons by their modern names. Therefore, using modern names facilitates comparison and reference to the contemporary counterparts of Galen’s descriptions. Second, because of the nature of the descriptions examined, which include some in Greek and some in Arabic translation, using the original name from the text for each body part will create an inconvenient inconsistency of mixed Arab-Greek terminology. Where necessary, the original name will be provided and discussed. If the name is in Arabic, a cross-reference to its Greek name from elsewhere in the Galenic corpus will be provided. The format of modern terms will be the English standard term or its official technical term (in modern Latin) when a common English term does not exist.
Anatomy of the Phonatory System
Framework
The organs of phonation lie within a supportive framework. The framework provides cover and smooth movement of the internal organs. The dissection of the anatomy of the phonatory system in AA.11 starts with the removal of a thin, paired muscle called ‘the muscular carpet’. The muscle serves as an active part of the phonatory system, as well as a muscular tissue that covers other structures within the system. Beneath it, lying structures such as muscles connecting the larynx and the hyoid bone to the sternum. Another reference to the muscular carpet, which is essential to phonation and modulation of voice, can be found in On Dissection of Muscles. There, the muscle is named by Galen and said to be tense when “cynic spasms” occur. This condition can affect voice production by the spread of the spasms to the larynx and pharynx, causing breathing difficulties and articulation problems. The “Cynic spasms” might correspond to the condition we call today “risus sardonicus”. Besides the muscular carpet, various fasciae protect the organs of the phonatory system. Galen notes that other anatomists have inadvertently destroyed the stylohyoid muscles while attempting to separate structures in the larynx area from the fasciae. 
Beneath these muscular and Fascial coverings lies another kind of envelope: the continuous mucous membrane. A significant observation by Galen is the continuous mucous membrane lining that extends from the oral cavity to the pharynx, descends to the lung through the larynx and trachea, and to the stomach via the oesophagus. This observation of continuity in the mucosal lining demonstrates Galen's ability to trace anatomical connections across different regions of the body. Galen's observations extend to the specific regions of this mucous lining. He notes the mucosa of the laryngopharynx, which connects the epiglottis with the larynx, binding the two together. The mucosa of the oropharynx, while not directly described, is implied as part of this continuous mucosal lining. 
Another connective membrane mentioned in Galen’s detailed examination is the thyrohyoid membrane. Galen states that the same type of membrane that composes the continuous mucous membrane also serves as the connection between the hyoid bone and the thyroid cartilage, which is recognised as the thyrohyoid membrane. This fibrous membrane plays a crucial role in maintaining the structural integrity of the laryngeal complex; therefore, its identification is critical for understanding laryngeal anatomy and the larynx's placement within the body. Another internal connection that completes the framework of the phonatory system is the capsule of the cricothyroid joint. While not directly described as a capsule, Galen's discussion of the connection between the cricoid and thyroid cartilages implies the existence of the capsule of the cricothyroid joint. This capsule is essential for the articulation between these two crucial laryngeal cartilages. It is important to note that while identifying the capsule, Galen’s description is relatively brief, and for him, it is a mere σύνδεσμος, a connective tissue that keeps the structural integrity between two body parts. Those are the structures that form the framework of the phonatory system. Next, power and support structures will be introduced. These are mostly muscles and bones that suspend, stabilise, and move the larynx. 
Power and support structures
Among the power and support structures, the hyoid bone is a central skeletal element of the phonatory system; it serves as the upper boundary of the larynx, situated just above the thyroid cartilage and below the jaw. Numerous muscles are connected to the hyoid bone. According to Galen, the hyoid bone is called by some hyoid (ὑοειδές) because of its resemblance to the letter upsilon (υ) and by others lambdoid (λαμβδοειδές), since it resembles the letter lambda (Λ).  Galen seems to prefer the name “hyoid” in his writings. Three pairs of muscles are connected to its lesser cornua, described by Galen as “thin and lean as the edges of a stylus” (λεπτῶν τε καὶ ἰσχνῶν, ὡς γραφείου περάτων). The first pair are the omohyoid muscles, which are long and narrow. They draw the scapulae forward to the front of the neck. Galen notes the presence of a muscle on each side, emphasising their nature as paired organs. Another pair is the geniohyoid pair of muscles, which consists of two muscles that originate from the hyoid bone and attach to the lower jaw. They assist in the motion of the hyoid bone. The third pair is the stylohyoid pair of muscles: Galen takes care to mention this small muscle pair, which is under the muscular carpet. He warns the reader lest he destroy that small muscle while removing the muscular carpet. Galen notes that the stylohyoid muscles, along with the styloid processes and the geniohyoid muscles, were often overlooked by other anatomists due to their small size. Other anatomists have destroyed them while separating the structures in the larynx area from the fasciae. Galen also provides helpful topographical information, noting that it originates near the styloglossus muscle and inserts on the inferolateral border of the hyoid bone. This level of detail in describing spatial relationships between structures is a hallmark of Galen's anatomical work; the detailed topographical layout creates a clear image for his reader that may guide them in performing the same dissection. Using Galen’s detailed instructions, the reader can avoid the mistakes that other anatomists have made. 
Extending from the lower margin of the hyoid bone (and not the lesser cornua) are the sternohyoid muscles. These muscles reach the sternum, where their clavicles articulate. They hold back the hyoid bone, thereby providing stability to the upper larynx region. Moreover, they are identified as long, flat strap muscles that lie superficially to the smaller sternothyroid muscles. One more pair of muscles extends from the hyoid bone: the thyrohyoid muscles. The thyrohyoid muscles belong to a group of eight muscles (four pairs) to which Galen assigns a name; The other eight muscles (ἑτέρων ὀκτώ).  Those muscles are opposed to the twelve special muscles (lit. “twelve to its [the larynx] own composition” κατὰ μὲν τὴν ἰδίαν αὐτοῦ σύνθεσιν δώδεκα). The eight muscles are extrinsic muscles of the larynx; they connect to it at different points and support it. The Thyrohyoid muscles connect the thyroid cartilage to the hyoid bone. They are described as moving the thyroid cartilage upward and forward. Thus, they allow the larynx to expand during phonation. 
Next, there are six additional muscles that belong to the same group as the other eight muscles. The sternothyroid muscle pair is identified and described as a pair of muscles that arise from the lower aspect of the hyoid bone and extend downwards towards the sternum. Similar to the sternohyoid muscles that hold back the hyoid bone, the sternothyroid muscles hold back the thyroid cartilage. In UP, Galen recognised these muscles as two in animals with a small larynx and as four in those with a larger one. To achieve the count of eight muscles, the sternothyroid muscles must be regarded as four. However, in the dissection of the larynx in AA, it is said explicitly that there are only two muscles, without considering the variants of animals with a large larynx. Although the division of special muscles and ‘muscles common to the larynx and its neighbouring structures’ (corresponding to the ‘eight others’) is mentioned, there is no numbering of the two muscle groups. Moreover, the principle of numbering of muscles is completely absent from the later books of AA. While leaving the numbering behind, the comparison between animals with a small larynx (the ape) and animals with a large one (the pig) does appear in the description in AA. Just before the naming of the two muscle groups, Galen says that the ape and the pig have the same number of “laryngeal muscles” (ʿaḍl al-ḥanjara), without saying the number. Therefore, it behoves us to assume that by laryngeal muscles, Galen means only the group of the twelve special muscles of the larynx and not the other eight, which can differ between six in apes, and eight in pigs. Nevertheless, another piece of evidence from UP suggests that Galen recognised a difference between apes and pigs, also within the group of the twelve special muscles: the pig is among the loud-voiced animals, which have four muscles between the thyroid and the cricoid cartilages, as opposed to just two. Consequently, the issue remains unresolved, revealing a genuine contradiction between the two treatises.
The last pair of muscles among the other eight muscles is the pharyngeal constrictor inferior pair of muscles. The constrictor pharynges inferior muscles are divided today into four separate muscles. However, Galen considers them two, uniting the thyropharyngeal part of the muscle with the cricopharyngeal, i.e., uniting the muscle that connects the thyroid cartilage to the pharynx with the muscle that connects the cricoid cartilage to the pharynx. The constrictor pharynges inferior are muscles which connect the thyroid and cricoid cartilages to the pharynx over the oesophagus and affix the larynx. Galen also notes that many anatomists believed these muscles to be part of the oesophagus. The next group of muscles is the twelve special muscles of the larynx, which consist of twelve intrinsic muscles that affect the movement of its cartilages during phonation. 
Muscles of the larynx: twelve special muscles
Among this group of twelve muscles, the thyroarytenoid muscles are the most crucial for phonation: Galen describes the thyroarytenoid muscles as arising from the lower thyroid cartilage and ascending obliquely to the arytenoid cartilage. The thyroarytenoid muscles are not visible until the larynx is opened. Galen considers those muscles to be the most important for voice production: they are connected to the glōttis and are responsible for closing it during voice production. Galen remarks that nature (φύσις) has demonstrated superb skill in constructing them and criticises other anatomists for missing them. Galen then describes several small but crucial muscles: the posterior cricoarytenoid muscles, which lie upon the cricoid cartilage, and the lateral cricoarytenoid muscles, which spring from the side of the cricoid cartilage. The cricoarytenoid muscles are responsible for opening the glottis. 
Moreover, there is the arytenoid muscle, consisting of oblique and transverse parts, which envelops the base of the arytenoid cartilages. Galen considers the two parts as two pairs of muscles. He notes the roles of the muscles in laryngeal function, such as enlarging the larynx sideways. According to Galen, they are only second in importance to the thyroarytenoid muscles. Two additional pairs of muscles that move the thyroid cartilage during phonation are the cricothyroid muscles, which connect the thyroid cartilage to the cricoid cartilage. Galen regarded the cricothyroid muscles as four distinct muscles, recognising two pairs corresponding to their oblique and straight parts. According to him, only in loud-voiced animals (ἐν τοῖς μεγαλοφώνοις ζῴοις), humans among them, possess these muscles in such a fourfold arrangement. The next group of body parts that will be examined is the nerves of the phonatory system. 
Nerves of the Phonatory System: Laryngeal nerves
There are two principal nerves in the phonatory system: the superior laryngeal nerve (SLN) and the recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN). While other nerves control other organs, which were said to be part of or auxiliary to the phonatory system, they pertain to those organs and do not control phonation directly. Moreover, given the extensive scope of Galen’s discussions of the nerves, encompassing a wide range of topics, this study will not address their activity in detail. Still, it will instead focus on their anatomical features and the significance Galen ascribes to them. In short, the nerves are operating the muscles, and any destruction of a specific nerve will destroy the activity of the muscle to which it is attached. The clearest example of that is when Galen says in his experiments on the muscles of the larynx that if you want to destroy the activity of a particular muscle, you can do it in one of two ways: inflict injury to the muscle itself or to the nerve which controls it. The nerves are of particular importance to Galen, particularly in his study of voice production. For him, the study of the RLN is connected to his personal journey of discovering the origin of the voice.
The recurrent laryngeal nerves (al-'aṣaba ar-rāji'a ilā fawq, τό παλινδρομοῦν νεῦρον) are described as two small nerves ascending from below, surrounded by sheaths (ʾagšiya) around the trachea, connecting to the larynx at the lower angles of the thyroid cartilage. Galen claims to have discovered the recurrent laryngeal nerve, since other anatomists had destroyed it during the dissection of the area.  The course of the laryngeal nerves is from the brain into the larynx, as they are branching from the Vagus (the sixth pair). This makes it one of the cranial nerves. The fact that this nerve is a cranial nerve is essential for Galen, as it demonstrates the strong connection between the brain and voice production. It is not fitting that the nerve controlling most of the muscles of the main vocal instrument, the larynx, would reach from the spine. In fact, Galen names it ‘unjust’ (ἄδικον) that this nerve would not reach from the brain, and he praises nature for constructing it the way it is. The recurrent laryngeal nerve controls almost all of the muscles of the larynx, except for the cricothyroid. The SLN controls the cricothyroid muscles. The SLNs are located adjacent to the carotid arteries. The SLN pair is divided by Galen to two nerve-shoots (al-šuʿab), that correspond to the internal and external branches of the SLN in modern anatomy. So far, the muscles and the nerves of the phonatory system have been described. Muscles and nerves represent two distinct yet interrelated categories of bodily structures: the nerves serve to activate and control the muscles. However, at the core of the phonatory system lies another essential component, neither muscular nor neural in nature, without which voice cannot be produced: the larynx. The next section of this chapter will be dedicated to the anatomy of the larynx.
The Larynx
The larynx is a structure composed mainly of three components: three laryngeal cartilages, the epiglottis, and the glōttis. While it has many muscles attached to it (as demonstrated earlier) and its own nerves, the muscles and the nerves are not considered by Galen as the larynx itself, but rather the muscles and nerves of the larynx. The thyroid cartilage is the biggest cartilage of the three that compose the larynx. It is described as a large, shield-shaped cartilage in the front of the larynx. Galen notes its flexibility and slightly convex surface, observations that contribute to understanding its role in phonation.  He also identifies a crest-like structure on the outer surface of the thyroid cartilage, now known as the crista cartilaginis thyroideae, extending from the mid-point of its upper margin to the lower portion and dividing the cartilage into two connected halves. The thyroid cartilage is the only cartilage extending above the opening of the glōttis [rima glottidis], meaning that it is the main cartilage that has an impact on modified air after its movement in the narrowing glōttis. The cricoid cartilage is described as positioned behind and connected to the thyroid cartilage, with its two sides affixing to the vertical sides of the thyroid cartilage. It is said to have a circular shape, like a ring. This description aligns well with modern anatomical understanding of the laryngeal framework. 
The two cartilages examined so far have shown no differences between Galen’s descriptions and the modern anatomical understanding. This is not the case for the arytenoid cartilage(s): Galen mentions the arytenoid cartilage as one rather than two separate structures. The term "arytenoid" derives its etymology from the Ancient Greek words ἀρύταινα, denoting "jug," and εἶδος (eidos), meaning "form.” The analogy derives from the resemblance between the pouring lip of a jug and the apex of the arytenoid cartilages in the pig. In this species, the arytenoid cartilages are fused superiorly into an angular, winding apex. According to modern anatomical studies, this configuration in the pig may contribute to a more effective closure of the larynx during swallowing. For Galen, this cartilage holds high importance since it is connected to the “parts of the glōttis”, ensuring the narrowing of the passage (rima glottidis) in voice production.

Illustration 1: The arytenoid cartilages in human beings (left), a reconstruction of Galen’s description of the arytenoid cartilage, based on pig anatomy (right), anterior view  
Another cartilage, which is part of the larynx, is the epiglottis. The epiglottis is described as a structure suspended or attached in the laryngeal orifice, connected to the larynx by the mucous membrane. It is described as rounded, cartilaginous, and a bit larger than the orifice of the larynx, facing towards the oesophagus. According to Galen, the cartilaginous composition of the epiglottis serves the utility that it could open during respiration as well as be shut while swallowing food. However, for Galen, the epiglottis is not closed completely: there is a small opening left between the epiglottis and the thyroid cartilage. In such a case, the epiglottis serves as a regulator, allowing a small amount of water to enter through the larynx and trachea into the lungs to moisten these organs. To the epiglottis is attached a muscle which we may call the “hyoepiglottic muscle”: Galen identifies a single, unpaired muscle, which springs from the hyoid bone's end, directs towards the tongue, and blends with the back of the epiglottis. The muscle varies between different animal species, with some animals having this muscle folded into two layers. It might be the hypoepiglottic muscle, which is apparent in felines, canines, horses, and pigs. In humans, it would be the hyoepiglottic ligament. This muscle is also present in UP, where it is noted that both a ligament and a muscular connector exist between the hyoid bone and the epiglottis. Moreover, in the same sentence, a non-existent muscular connection between the hyoid bone and the pharynx is mentioned. Therefore, the text might be corrupt here. In my interpretation, I tend to see the connection between the hyoid bone and the epiglottis as a muscular connection since it aligns both with the description in AA and with the anatomical state of the connection in the pig, on which Galen bases his other prominent laryngeal anatomical claims (e.g., the anatomy of the arytenoid cartilage). The remaining component of the larynx is the glōttis. The glōttis is the inner part of the larynx. It requires special attention because it is where voice emerges. 
Although it is the focal point of voice production, the glōttis is described in only one extensive account across all of Galen’s treatises (excluding De Voce). The thirteenth chapter of the seventh book of UP is dedicated entirely to the glōttis, covering its anatomy and physiology. Aside from this chapter, there are six brief mentions in the later books of AA of what we could assume is the glōttis: it is mentioned by the name al-ghulṣamah, which is the epiglottis. Nevertheless, the extensive account of the glōttis in UP. 7.13 makes it clear that the glōttis is a unique structure in the body, meticulously designed for voice production. Galen describes the glōttis as “a certain body” (τι σῶμα) located within the inner cavity of the larynx, through which pneuma passes inwards and upwards: 
Περὶ μὲν δὴ τῶν μυῶν τε καὶ τῶν χόνδρων τοῦ λάρυγγος εἴρηται· περὶ δὲ τῶν ἄλλων ἐφεξῆς λεγέσθω. κατὰ τὴν ἔνδον χώραν αὐτοῦ, δι’ ἧς εἴσω τε καὶ ἔξω τὸ πνεῦμα φέρεται, τέτακταί τι σῶμα, περὶ οὗ μικρὸν ἔμπροσθεν εἶπον, οὔτε τὴν οὐσίαν, οὔτε τὸ σχῆμα παραπλήσιον ἑτέρῳ τινὶ τῶν καθ’ ὅλον τὸ ζῶον. ὑπὲρ οὗ λέλεκται μέν μοι κᾀν τοῖς περὶ φωνῆς, ἐπιδεικνύντι τὸ πρῶτόν τε καὶ κυριώτατον ὑπάρχειν αὐτὸ τῆς φωνῆς ὄργανον· εἰρήσεται δὲ καὶ νῦν, ὅσον εἰς τὰ παρόντα ἐστὶ χρήσιμον. 
The muscles and the cartilages of the larynx were indeed described: it shall be spoken now about the other parts in what follows. In its (the larynx’s) inner cavity, through which pneuma passes in and out, is fixed a certain body, on which I shortly spoke before, that resembles neither in substance nor in structure to any of the other body parts in all of the body. I have also spoken about it in my treatise On The Voice, showing that it is the first and the most proper organ of the voice, and it will be said now, as much as is necessary for the present affairs.
Gal. UP.7.13, 407,19 – 26 Helmreich = 3.560 – 561 Kühn 
Galen’s examination of this structure is particularly striking due to its uniqueness. He describes the glottis explicitly as a ‘certain body' (τι σῶμα). Galen is emphatic, stating that “it does not resemble in substance (οὐσία) nor in structure (σχῆμα) to any of the other body parts in all of the body”. This is not a casual remark; such explicit differentiation is rare in his writings: as we saw until now, most of the described organs were described in such a way that they could be divided into groups with the organs of each group having a common anatomical feature. Moreover, it underscores its physiological importance and the inherent difficulty in visualising it. The ‘certain body’ has not been given a specific name yet. From the upcoming description, it will be evident that Galen is the one who assigns it its name. The lemma γλωττίς nor a description of the organ appear anywhere else before Galen’s account. In the following sections of the chapter, Galen takes us to the naming of the “Glōttis” through an analogy from the musical semantic field: 
ἔοικε μὲν οὖν αὐλοῦ γλώττῃ, μάλιστα κάτωθέν τε καὶ ἄνωθεν αὐτὸ θεωμένῳ. λέγω δὲ κάτωθεν μὲν, ἵνα συνάπτουσιν ἀλλήλοις ἥ τ’ ἀρτηρία καὶ ὁ λάρυγξ· ἄνωθεν δὲ, κατὰ τὸ στόμα τὸ γεννώμενον ὑπὸ τῶν ταύτῃ περάτων τοῦ τε ἀρυταινοειδοῦς χόνδρου καὶ τοῦ θυρεοειδοῦς. ἄμεινον δ’ ἦν ἄρα μὴ τοῦτο τὸ σῶμα ταῖς τῶν αὐλῶν γλώτταις εἰκάζειν, ἀλλ’ ἐκείνας τῷδε.
It resembles the mouthpiece (glōttis) of an aulos (αὐλός), especially when it is viewed from below or above. I mean below, where the (rough) artery and the larynx meet [Cricotracheal ligament]: I mean above, through the orifice that is formed by the borders of the arytenoid and thyroid cartilages. It would be better not to liken that body to the mouthpieces of auloi, but rather to liken the mouthpieces to that body.
Gal. UP. 7.13, 407,26-408,7 Helmreich = 3.561 Kühn
To articulate the exceptional character of this body part, Galen turns to a vivid analogy. In the passage above, he states that the glōttis “resembles…the mouthpiece (glōttis) of an aulos”: Ἔοικε μὲν αὐλοῦ γλώττῃ. Later, he uses the verb εἰκάζειν to liken the mouthpieces of the auloi to the glōttis. The use of εἰκάζειν, coming from the same semantic field as εἰκών, image, reveals Galen’s intention to evoke a concrete mental image: the reader is invited to visualise a hidden anatomical structure through a familiar cultural artefact. The aulos, a prominent double-reeded instrument in antiquity, is crucial here. It offers a model by which the glōttis can be conceived as a structure that regulates the movement of air to produce sound. Yet Galen is careful to delimit the scope of the comparison. Before introducing the analogy, he insists that the glōttis “resembles neither in substance nor in structure to any of the other body parts in all of the body.” The comparison thus serves an explanatory and heuristic purpose, not an ontological one – it helps to conceptualise what cannot be seen, without reducing the organ to its image. 
This rhetorical and explanatory strategy is rooted in a long-standing scientific tradition. As demonstrated by Geoffrey E.R. Lloyd, the use of analogy in Greek science progressed from straightforward visual resemblances to complex functional models that sought causal relationships, reflecting a gradual increase in analytical sophistication, culminating in Aristotelian thought. Galen’s analogy exemplifies what Lloyd identifies in Aristotle as functional analogies: analogies grounded in shared causal structures, rather than just appearance. Such analogies were used in his biological treatises. By distinguishing similarity in activity (sound production) from mere shape, Galen presents the glōttis as the “first and most proper organ of voice,” defined by function rather than form.
Von Staden has shown that Galen allows the use of metaphor as a didactic aid when literal language alone cannot provide explicit instruction. In De Pulsuum differentiis, Galen states that metaphor may be employed for the sale of instruction and not mere talk, a principle that von Staden interprets as part of his broader theory of scientific language. For Galen, initial teaching requires proper names (ὀνόματα κύρια). Still, once those are established, analogy may serve as a concise clarifier for the already initiated, provided it does not distort meaning. The aulos comparison belongs precisely to the pedagogical context: it is not a lapse into figurative ornament but a controlled instrument of explanation. Likewise, Chiara Ferella characterises metaphors in early medical writing as ‘deliberate metaphors’, consciously employed to investigate and demonstrate hidden processes. In her analysis of Hippocratic and Empedoclean analogies, she indicates that comparisons with familiar, visible phenomena serve not only to illustrate but also to test and confirm theoretical hypotheses, representing an early form of heuristic reasoning. These deliberate metaphors compel the audience to attend to the source domain as the field of observation in which inference and demonstration occur, rather than to the hidden parts of the human body. The use of these deliberate metaphors underpins the epistemological model employed by Hippocratic physicians in their efforts to explore what lies beyond observation in the superficial, visible layer of human beings.
Galen’s aulos analogy operates partly in this way. By projecting the visible and culturally familiar mechanism of sound production in the aulos onto the concealed operation of the glōttis, he renders the process intelligible. However, unlike the Hippocratic physicians and Empedocles, Galen, in his accounts, actively encourages his readers to explore the hidden parts of the body through dissection. In sum, unlike earlier medical tradition, Galen does not want his readers to devote themselves entirely to likening body parts and processes in the human body to the visible, external phenomena. For him, the analogy serves as a didactic tool that has defined limits. The limits are determined by him clearly by stating that the two objects are not equal: as will be demonstrated, the glōttis is superior to the mouthpiece of the aulos.

Galen refines this comparison: He argues it would be ‘better not to compare that body to the mouthpieces of auloi, but rather to compare the mouthpieces to those bodies.’ This sentence precedes his argument in the following sections: In essence, the aulos’s mouthpiece is a human invention (τέχνη) that imitates nature’s (φύσις) original and superior design of the glōttis. This positions the glōttis as a perfect model of natural design. This sequence is vital: anatomical discovery prompted Galen to draw upon a familiar cultural reference for explanation, not the other way around. Galen concludes the introduction to his analogy by asserting two points: First, nature holds the original, perfect design of the structure, while humankind furnished a mechanism that copied the natural design imperfectly. Second, and more relevant to the physiological process itself: as the aulos cannot produce sound without its mouthpiece, so is the human body that cannot phonate without the glōttis.
καὶ γὰρ καὶ πρότερον, οἶμαι, τῷ χρόνῳ καὶ σοφώτερον τοῖς ἔργοις ἡ φύσις τῆς τέχνης ἐστίν. ὥστ’, εἴπερ τουτὶ μὲν τὸ σῶμα φύσεως ἔργον ὑπάρχει, τέχνης δ’εὕρημά ἐστι τὸ κατὰ τοὺς αὐλοὺς, ἐκεῖνο τούτου μίμημα ἂν εἴη, πρὸς ἀνδρὸς εὑρημένον σοφοῦ, γνωρίζειν τε καὶ μιμεῖσθαι τὰ τῆς φύσεως ἔργα δυναμένου. ὅτι μὲν οὖν χωρὶς τῆς γλώττης ἄχρηστος ὁ αὐλὸς, αὐτὸ δείκνυσι τὸ φαινόμενον. αἰτίαν δ’ οὐ χρὴ ποθεῖν ἀκούειν ἐν τῷ παρόντι λόγῳ. λέλεκται γὰρ ἐν τῇ περὶ φωνῆς πραγματείᾳ, καθ’ ἣν καὶ τοῦτο εὐθὺς ἀποδέδεικται, τὸ μὴ δύνασθαι γενέσθαι φωνὴν ἄνευ τοῦ στενωθῆναι τὴν διέξοδον.
For I think nature is prior in time and wiser in actions than the ‘craft’. Therefore, this body (glōttis) is a deed of nature, and that which in the aulos is an invention of skill, that (the invention) would be an imitation of it (the body), invented by a wise man who is able to understand and imitate the deeds of nature. Well, the aulos is useless without its mouthpiece; the evidence proves that. There is no need to yearn to hear the reason in this work, for it was said in the treatise On the Voice, where it was straightforwardly demonstrated that it is not possible to generate a voice without the narrowing of the passage.
Gal. UP. 7.13, 408.7-18 Helmreich = 561 Kühn
This passage illustrates how Galen articulates an account that explores the interplay between nature and human skill: a wise man, after noticing nature’s invention, creates the aulos with its double-reeded mechanism in an act of imitation (what we would call today biomimicry). Galen does not explicitly state here why the glōttis is better than the aulos’ mouthpiece. It would be explained later in his description, as he will connect the anatomy of the glōttis and its complex mechanism. In her work, Rebecca Flemming analyses the concept of the alternately labelled ‘nature’ (φύσις) and ‘demiurge’ (δημιουργός) in Galen’s medical and philosophical systems. Galen’s conception of the demiurge emerges early in his intellectual career and continues to appear throughout his corpus, ultimately becoming a central and organising principle in UP. Per Flemming, the fact that nature is also referred to as demiurge, ‘craftsman’, emphasises that nature stands above humanity, as the craftsman stands above its craft. Moreover, nature is defined by having both ‘skill’ (τέχνη) as well as ‘forethought’ (πρόνοια). Returning to our passage, we see that the human inventor possesses skill but lacks foresight. Therefore, his capacity is limited to an imitation of nature.
Special attention is given here to a prominent property of the mouthpiece of the aulos: its narrowing. The narrowing passage is deemed crucial for voice production. The narrowing of the passage is indeed vital: there must be a narrowing of the passage in the glōttis. This requirement is known today as the application of Bernoulli’s law to the pressure in the glōttis. Galen, of course, was not aware of Bernoulli’s equation. However, he does see the narrowing of the space inside the glōttis when the two parts of the glōttis unfold. A passage in On the Voice provides a sufficient explanation: the narrowing of the channel increases the air’s speed, which makes the voice stronger. The narrowness Galen describes is the narrowness that allows the two vocal folds (or the two reeds of the aulos's mouthpiece) to be close enough to each other for vibration to occur. For Galen, the narrowness itself is sufficient as it comes as a preliminary stage to the striking of the air by the cartilages, which is the moment of the production of voice, as it will be explained in detail later on in this work. 
The glōttis consists of two parts, left and right (εἰς ταὐτὸν γὰρ αὐτῆς ἔρχεται τὰ μόρια, τό τ’ ἐκ τῶν ἀριστερῶν καὶ τὸ τῶν δεξιῶν), which meet to close the passage [rima glottidis] accurately. These correspond to the vestibular folds (false vocal folds), which in pigs (Galen’s main vivisection model) overlie the true vocal folds with an intervening cavity. Beneath each part of the glōttis lies a considerable inner cavity (τρῆμα καθ’ ἑκάτερον μέρος τῆς γλωττίδος ἓν ἐργασαμένῃ): the laryngeal ventricle. It remains closed during normal breathing but is opened when the passage is constricted for phonation. When the pneuma is thrust forcefully upward, these cavities fill with air, thus helping regulate the volume of air entering the passage, ensuring that the parts of the glōttis are not forced to open upwards and the opening of the whole glōttis stays narrowed. Structurally, each part is elongated along the ventricle’s aperture vertically, “like a narrow line” (γραμμὴ στενή). Still, it is not really narrow: it appears as a ‘wrinkle’ (ῥυσότης) because the membranous substance of these lips falls down, creating this unique shape. The glōttis is composed of membranous material, giving it both durability and flexibility to endure the opposing states of the larynx: open during respiration and narrowed during phonation. Nature has further equipped it with a unique moistening fluid, sticky (γλίσχρον) and fatty (λιπαρὸν), which retains moisture for a far longer period than watery fluid, preventing drying and ensuring proper function. The absence of such fluid would cause rapid drying and loss of voice, as seen in fevers or heat exposure. This section has focused on Galen’s anatomical description of the glōttis, detailing its structure. In the next chapter of this work, attention will shift from anatomy to physiology, addressing the mechanisms by which the glōttis operates during phonation and transforms pneuma into voice. These mechanisms rely not only on the glōttis, but also on the other body parts of the phonatory system described above. 
Conclusions
The detailed examination of Galen's anatomical descriptions demonstrates that while he did not explicitly name a "phonatory system," he clearly had in mind an integrated anatomical complex dedicated to voice production. The systematic documentation of structures from the superficial muscular carpet to the deep laryngeal components reveals a sophisticated understanding of how these various parts collaborate to enable the physiological process of phonation. The analysis has revealed several key aspects of Galen's anatomical work. Galen demonstrates a meticulous and methodical approach, examining each part and allowing him to progress from superficial to deeper structures. This approach demonstrates careful consideration for both practical dissection and a thorough understanding of the anatomical parts, as well as pedagogical clarity. His precise descriptions of spatial relationships between structures, particularly evident in his treatment of the laryngeal complex, demonstrate an understanding that goes beyond mere cataloguing of parts. Notably, his discovery of structures such as the recurrent laryngeal nerve and his careful documentation of small muscles often overlooked by his contemporaries (according to him) allow him to demonstrate both his technical skill and his commitment to thorough investigation. 
Galen's meticulous, methodical approach to anatomical inquiry is grounded in a firmly established medical-philosophical thought: he is driven to explore the human body, considering humankind as the craftwork of a skilled craftsman, nature. While human craftsmen are distinct in terms of ‘skill’ (τέχνη), they cannot reach the level of nature that crafts both with skill and ‘forethought’ (πρόνοια). Therefore, in his inquiries, Galen operates within a teleological mindset, holding that each body part is designed to serve its function within the body. As will be demonstrated in the next chapter, this way of thinking helped Galen craft complex physiological theories that integrated his novel anatomical achievements. This integration may be regarded as laying the foundations of functional anatomy. This analysis not only illuminates an essential chapter in the history of anatomy but also reveals how fundamental aspects of our understanding of voice production were first systematically documented. Galen's work represents a significant development from earlier anatomical traditions. While building upon foundations laid in the Hippocratic corpus, such as the examination of different qualities of voice, and by anatomists like Rufus of Ephesus, who explored the role of nerves, Galen's approach was more comprehensive and systematic. His integration of previous knowledge with his own discoveries, particularly evident in his understanding (and his ability to demonstrate it) of muscular and nervous system components, as well as his discoveries of unique structures such as the glottis, marks a crucial development in ancient medical knowledge. The detailed attention he paid to the continuity of structures, such as the mucosal system, reveals an appreciation for anatomical interconnections. The next chapter will discuss Galen’s physiological theory of voice production. Naturally, the next chapter will rely heavily on the anatomical basis established in this chapter: the physiological process of voice production is the manifestation of the relationships among the body parts described in this chapter.






Chapter 2 – Physiology
Introduction
Having established the anatomical framework of the phonatory system in the previous chapter, this chapter examines how these structures work together to transform breath into voice. This chapter analyses Galen’s physiological explanation of voice production, focusing on the transformation of forceful exhalation (ἐκφύσησις) into voice (φωνή) as it moves through the trachea into the larynx. The discussion begins by identifying what Galen means by ἐκφύσησις, which provides the suitable material of voice (ὕλη τις οἰκεία φωνῆς). The mechanics of the larynx act upon this material. From this point, attention will shift to the mechanisms of the larynx: the interaction between ἐκφύσησις and the cartilages, the narrowing of the glōttis, and the coordinated activity of the muscles and nerves that regulate these movements. This chapter aims to reconstruct Galen’s account of phonation as a dynamic sequence of physical events, clarifying the mechanisms by which air is modified to produce voice. The analysis will not address the ontological relation between air and voice as distinct kinds of matter: whether air itself is transformed into a different material substance when it becomes voice. Such a question of material conversion belongs to a metaphysical level of inquiry that lies beyond the scope of the present discussion. Instead, the focus here is strictly physiological: how ἐκφύσησις interacts with the anatomical structures of the phonatory system (with particular emphasis on the larynx) and how their coordinated motion, narrowing, and their effects on it generate voice. Two further topics also remain outside the scope of the present discussion. The first concerns the initiation of phonation in the brain: whether it is voluntary or involuntary, and how the brain communicates with the organs of voice. The second concerns the outcome of phonation and its further refinement: what occurs after the emission of voice, namely its articulation and transformation into speech, processes that involve additional organs and neural functions beyond the phonatory system itself. Having defined the limits of this inquiry, the following analysis focuses on the physiological sequence itself and its underlying principles, with particular emphasis on events in the larynx. In tracing these stages, the chapter demonstrates that for Galen, phonation is not a spontaneous by-product of respiration but a well-coordinated interaction between structures and their motion, in which the larynx functions as the pivotal site of transformation. 
The discussion proceeds in two stages. The first section serves as a necessary introduction to the analysis of the phonation process. It defines ἐκφύσησις and establishes its role as the proper material of voice, a type of exhalation prepared for phonation. The section will examine how ἐκφύσησις, forceful exhalation, is differentiated by Galen from regular exhalation (ἐκπνοή), and why the latter is not sufficient to become voice. It will be demonstrated that Galen regards ἐκφύσησις as activity (ἐνέργεια) and has the quality of a degree of intensity. The second section examines the operation of the laryngeal mechanism as a single, coordinated system. By laryngeal mechanism, I mean the larynx, the special muscles of the larynx, and the laryngeal nerves. Other parts of the phonatory system play secondary roles, which were discussed in the last chapter and will be addressed again in greater detail if needed. The second section will show how the cartilages of the larynx, moved by their respective muscles and directed by the laryngeal nerves, act upon the ἐκφύσησις through impact (πληγή). Galen illustrates this effect through an analogy drawn once again from music: the impact of the cartilage on the air is compared to that of a plectrum striking a string.
Galen’s theory of impact bears a striking resemblance to those of Aristotle and the Stoics in explaining the production of sound. Moreover, Galen’s analogy will raise an important question; the answer to it will help in understanding the physiology of voice production, with an emphasis on the role of the glōttis: if the impact of cartilages on ἐκφύσησις is causing voice, why can voice emerge only when reaching the larynx, and not earlier, in the trachea? The trachea has a cartilaginous part, and ἐκφύσησις moves through it before reaching the larynx. As will be demonstrated, the interactions of cartilages and ἐκφύσησις by themselves are not sufficient to produce voice. Galen adds a critical preliminary step in the sequence of phonation in the description of the glōttis, the modification of ἐκφύσησις in the glōttis before reaching the cartilages. An integrative analysis combining close reading in passages from the description of the cartilages of the larynx in PHP, the description of the glōttis in UP, and the physiology of phonation in De Voce will show that the ἐκφύσησις that enters the larynx is modified with an increase of speed by going through the narrowed passage of the glōttis, before reaching the cartilages. The integrative analysis will attempt to fill the explanatory gap in Galen’s accounts and suggest that voice arises from the cartilages' impact on a more forceful ἐκφύσησις, which is modified in the glōttis. After the voice is created in this way, the cartilages, both of the larynx and the trachea, continue to resonate the emitted voice. This interpretation of Galen’s physiology of phonation addresses the question raised in the second section, incorporates all components of the phonatory system as having a clear role in the process, and presents a comprehensive teleological epistemic model that aligns with the Galenic model demonstrated throughout his accounts. 
Galen’s account is much more than an anatomical correction of his predecessors: it is a significant epistemological turning point in humankind’s studies of voice. Voice arises not from a particular place (such as the heart) but from process. The process depends on both physical forces and anatomical structures, rather than solely on volition and meaning. By tracing these requirements and conditions for phonation, this chapter demonstrates how Galen constructs a systematic model of voice production based on complex anatomical inquiry, physiological precision, a high level of detail, and philosophically ambitious considerations. This systemic model, however, stands in sharp contrast to the fragmented explanation of Galen’s predecessors, who did not inquire into the integrated nature of voice production. Galen identifies the material, muscular movements induced by nerves, and other structures and mechanisms that work together to achieve phonation. He criticises earlier thinkers, saying they tended to isolate voice to singular interactions between organs, such as the tongue and the teeth, or the passage of air through the mouth or nose. What they lacked, according to Galen, was the recognition that phonation is not reducible to one point of contact or merely the direction of airflow. Instead, it depends on the coordinated action of deeper, often unseen, structures that generate these interactions (e.g., the larynx) and control them (e.g., the laryngeal muscles). In this context, Galen launches his critique of prior theories of voice, which, in his view, rest on superficial observations without anatomical or physiological grounding.  
In the following polemic passage, we can observe how Galen criticises his predecessors for being unaware of these complexities: 
Οἱ γὰρ ἐπιχειρήσαντες εἰπεῖν τι περὶ τῶν στοιχειωδῶν φωνῶν ἄχρι τοσοῦτον προῆλθον ὡς ἀποφῆνασθαι τήνδε μέν τινα γίγνεσθαι στηριζομένης τῆς γλώττης πρὸς τοὺς τομέας ὀνομαζομένους ὀδόντας, ἤτοι τοὺς κατὰ τὴν ἄνω γένυν ἢ τὴν κάτω, τὴν δὲ πρὸς τὸν οὐρανίσκον ἀνακλωμένης ἢ πρὸς ἄλλο τι μόριον, ὥσπερ γε καὶ τὸ ἀναφερόμενον ἐκ τοῦ λάρυγγος πνεῦμα ποτε μὲν ἐπὶ τὰς εἰς τὴν ῥῖνα συντρήσεις ἀφικνεῖσθαι, ποτε δ’ ἀνεῳγμένου τοῦ στόματος ἐκφυσᾶσθαι, καὶ ποτε μὲν ἀθρόον καὶ πολύ, ποτε δ’ ἐλαττόν τε καὶ κατὰ βραχύ. τοὺς δὲ τὰς κινήσεις ταύτας ἐργαζομένους μῦς οὐδεὶς οὐδὲ τούτων εἶπεν, ὅπου γε οὐδὲ τοῖς ἐπὶ πλεῖστον ἀνατομικῆς ἐμπειρίας ἀφικομένοις εὕρηνται πω βεβαίως.
For those who attempted to say something about the elemental voices up to have gone so far to declare that a confident voice is produced from the tongue being fixated against the teeth called incisors, either those of the upper jaw or the lower, or when it [the tongue] is bent back towards the palate or towards some other part, just as the breath coming from the larynx sometimes reaches the passages (that go) into the nose [nasal fossae], and sometimes it is blown out through the open mouth, in one case in a massed and large current, in another in a lesser and smaller one. None of them mentioned the muscles that perform these movements, in the place where (the muscles) could not be found firmly by those who practised dissections systematically. 
Gal. De Foet. Form. 6.26, 100-102,25-9 Nickel = 4.697 Kühn 
As this passage shows, Galen criticises those who attempt to explain the production of voice without awareness of the movements of the muscles that operate the organs of voice production and articulation. First, he criticises them for studying articulated voices (the voice after production, modified by the palate, teeth, etc.) while attempting to study the production of voice itself. Galen uses στοιχειωδῶν φωνῶν (elemental voices) to describe the “simple voices” they sought to study. The use of στοιχειώδης can serve both to describe a simple sound of speech and to express a polemical stance against the Stoics, who considered στοιχεῖα the basic principles to be taught. As mentioned earlier, the Stoics did not focus primarily on the basic principles of voice emission in itself, but rather on the distinctions among voice, syllables, speech, and related concepts. The use of στοιχειωδῶν here may suggest Galen’s criticism of them for not understanding the most fundamental stage of speech: emission of voice. Second, for him, one cannot comprehend such a physiological process without grasping the bigger picture: it is not possible to reduce the process into one organ nor to the mere interaction between several organs, but instead one should study the supporting organs that allow these interactions. The given example concerns identifying the muscles. 
Crucial to understanding Galen’s approach is his systemic view of the body: unlike some of his predecessors, Galen insists that no single organ acts independently to produce voice. In PHP, Galen targets philosophical schools that argue that the heart is the source of voice production, given its central position, making it the most capable organ to be the seat of the soul. Particularly significant is the fact that Galen’s refutations of this argument, and of the philosophers who promoted it, did not remain confined to polemical writing but were dramatically demonstrated before them and before the wider Roman public through an impressive act of vivisection. This demonstration influenced Galen’s later career: Flavius Boethus, who invited Galen to perform the demonstration, also encouraged Galen later in his career to write UP. Galen dismisses the idea that the voice is “sent out from the heart,” arguing instead that what travels through the windpipe (trachea) is not yet voice but forceful exhalation (ἐκφύσησις), which is later transformed into voice in the larynx, which is composed and operated by nerves, muscles, cartilages, membranous tissues, and more. The larynx is indeed the place in which voice emerges. Still, Galen is not offering a mere correction of anatomical location, but an advancement of a broader epistemological principle: voice is a process, not a product of static position. It arises from the motion, modulation, and interaction of numerous structures. Firstly, it requires a suitable material. For Galen, it is forceful exhalation – ἐκφύσησις.
Ἐκφύσησις – a suitable material for voice
This section aims to clarify what the term ἐκφύσησις means and to demonstrate that for Galen, this is the materialistic foundation of the process of voice production. The process of phonation can be described as a transformative process. The first stage in such a process is having an input, in the form of material, which could be modified: exhalation. Galen explicitly defines the input: 
ἔμπροσθεν δὲ οὐδέπω φωνὴ τὸ ἀναφερόμενόν ἐστιν, ἀλλ’ ὡς ἔφαμεν ὕλη τις οἰκεία φωνῆς, ἣν ὀνομάζομεν ἐκφύσησιν.
What rises beforehand is not yet voice, but, as we said, a kind of matter suitable for voice, which we call exhalation (ἐκφύσησιν).
Gal. PHP. 2.5.53, 138,10-13 De Lacy = 5.251 Kühn
As seen from the passage, ἐκφύσησις is not voice, but a “suitable matter” (ὕλη τις οἰκεία) that can be voice. The term ἐκφύσησις appears firstly in the Galenic corpus, occuring in it seventy-five times and nowhere else before him. The term is used to describe a more forceful type of exhalation compared to the regular ἐκπνοή, a form of passive exhalation. A closer examination of these two terms reveals the critical distinction Galen draws. Both words share the prefix ek-, indicating exhalation, yet ἐκφύσησις is derived from the verb φυσάω, meaning "to blow," and denotes active, intentional, muscular engagement. Conversely, ἐκπνοή originates from πνεύω, a verb denoting breathing in a broader, more general sense. Galen’s term ἐκφύσησις belongs to the morphological type of -σις action nouns, which typically retain a strongly processual meaning. This morphological behaviour corresponds to the physiological function that Galen assigns to it: an active, forceful exhalation, rather than a static product or channel of air. Galen does not suffice in the terminological nuances, and defines the difference between the two decisively:
… ἡ ἐκφύσησις ἐκπνοή τίς ἐστιν ἀθρόα καὶ σφοδρὰ… 
ἐκφύσησις is a kind of a sudden and vehement ἐκπνοή.
Gal. AA. 8.7, 529,20-21 Garofalo = 2.689 Kühn
The fundamental difference between these two forms of exhalation, essential to understanding Galen’s physiological explanation of voice production, lies precisely in the muscular movements involved. The difference in achieving ἐκφύσησις instead of ἐκπνοὴ comes from different muscular movements in the thoracic cavity: two different mentions define ἐκφύσησις as “sudden outward movement of breath, occurring through the action of the intercostal muscles”. A significant physiological difference is drawn between the two exhalations: ἐκφύσησις is an activity (ἐνέργεια), ἐκπνοὴ is defined as “relaxation and sort of rest from the activity of the thorax” (ἡ ἐκπνοὴ ἄνεσίς ἐστι καὶ οἷον ἀνάπαυσις τῆς ἐνεργείας τοῦ θώρακος ἡ ἐκφύσησις δὲ ἐνέργεια). Moreover, ἐκφύσησις can have the quality of intensity (τὸ μᾶλλόν τε καὶ ἧττον ἔχει), and essentially differs from ἐκπνοὴ in the quantity and speed of movement (διαφέρουσα πλήθει καὶ τάχει τῆς κινήσεως). The two exhalation types are not codependent: the ability to produce ἐκφύσησις can be lost while ἐκπνοὴ is preserved. Such a state may occur from damaging the intercostal muscles: 
Dixit Galienus: Si neruis qui sunt inter costas acciderit scissio prope spondilia, destruetur uox et hanelitus.
Galen said: if to the nerves, which are in between the ribs, a scission near the vertebrae happens, the voice and exhalation will be destroyed. 
De Voce 1.1. Nutton.
The opening of the treatise On Voice highlights the importance of the intercostal muscles, whose connection to exhalation and voice production is demonstrated by the fact that, if the nerves that control these muscles are cut, voice and exhalation are lost. Based on the context in which the term appears throughout the treatise, hanelitus is the Latin equivalent of the Greek term ἐκφύσησις. If a state of loss of ἐκφύσησις occurs, as it happens in the impairment of the intercostal muscles, three functions are lost:
εἰ δ' ἡ μὲν ἐκπνοὴ σώζοιτο, διαφθαρείη δὲ ἡ ἐκφύσησις, αἱ λοιπαὶ τρεῖς ἐνέργειαι διαφθείρονται, ψοφώδης ἐκφύσησις καὶ φωνὴ καὶ διάλεκτος. 

If expiration is preserved but ἐκφύσησις is destroyed, the remaining three activities are destroyed: noisy ἐκφύσησις, voice, and speech.
Gal. De Loc. Aff. 3.9.8, 140,7-10 Wittwer = 8. 271-272 Kühn.
The loss of ἐκφύσησις means a loss of three ἐκφύσησις-based functions: noisy- ἐκφύσησις, voice production, and speech. Each function, as it is clearly demonstrated in Galen’s description of the phonation process, relies on the other. The fact that the loss of ἐκφύσησις alone can eliminate voice and speech, even if ordinary breathing remains intact, demonstrates that Galen viewed ἐκφύσησις as the foundation of all vocal activity. ἐκφύσησις is not merely a preparatory stage but a constitutive element of phonation, without which the body’s most articulate and rational expressions: voice and speech, cannot emerge. To conclude, Galen’s concept of ἐκφύσησις underscores the importance he attributes to detailed muscular coordination within the broader framework of physiological processes. The distinction between ἐκφύσησις and ἐκπνοὴ elucidates his holistic approach, emphasising the integration of anatomy, physiology, and philosophical thought. This integration exemplifies Galen's innovative perspective on voice production, emphasising complexity over simplicity and systemic interdependence over isolated interactions. The next step toward phonation would be the movement of ἐκφύσησις through the trachea and its arrival in the larynx. The trachea is crucial for voice production and is considered by Galen to be one of the three organs of phonation (with the larynx and the pharynx). For Galen, the lungs and their “rough arteries” (τραχείας ἀρτηρίας), namely the bronchi, are the beginning of phonation. They are not exclusive to the phonation system, but rather a structure that serves two activities: respiration (ἀναπνοῆ) and phonation (φωνῆ). The trachea was furnished to fulfil a dual function: channel for air in respiration (ἐκφύσησις or ἐκπνοὴ) as well as a component in phonation: channelling ἐκφύσησις for its modification in the larynx. The partly cartilaginous character of the trachea will be referred to in the next section, which will commence with an examination of the role of cartilages in phonation. The following section of this chapter examines the core of the phonation process, beginning with the role of the cartilages.
The shaping of ἐκφύσησις into voice 
Galen describes the cartilaginous material as the proper material to produce voice. Its role is to strike the ἐκφύσησις that reaches it. In his description of the trachea, Galen explains the utility of the cartilaginous material; the description starts with a passage described by Margaret Tallmadge May as a “cryptic sentence”: 
δέδεικται γὰρ ἐν τοῖς Περὶ φωνῆς ὑπομνήμασιν, ὡς οὐχ ἅπασα πληγὴ τοῦ ἀέρος ἱκανὴ φωνὴν ἀπεργάσασθαι, χρῆναι γὰρ δὴ συμμετρίαν ὑπάρχειν τινὰ τῆς τοῦ πλήττοντος οὐσίας τε ἅμα καὶ ῥώμης, ὡς ἐπ᾿ ὀλίγον ἀνθίστασθαι τὸν ἀέρα, καὶ μὴ κατὰ τὴν πρώτην ἐμβολὴν ἀνατραπῆναι νικηθέντα. 

For it was demonstrated in the treatise On the Voice, that not every strike of the air is sufficient to produce voice, for indeed there must be some proportionality between the substance of the striker as well as the force [of the thing struck], so that the air resists for a short time, and does not overcome, being conquered at the first charge. 
Gal. UP. 7.4, 379,10-16 Helmreich = 3.522 Kühn
	I accept May’s suggestion that something is missing from the sentence, and her addition “of the thing struck”. It is an appropriate addition: first, since ῥώμη is not usually used to denote the strength of materials, second, it will be demonstrated later how this passage aligns with an additional description of the striking, which will be discussed next. Before moving on, it seems that this passage argues that phonation occurs only when there is a certain proportionality between the air and the substance of that which strikes it. Galen proceeds and says that cartilages provide this proportionality in animals (καὶ ταύτην τὴν συμμετρίαν ὁ χόνδρος ἐν τοῖς ζῴοις κέκτηται). The expression “in animals” may at first appear redundant. Yet it is significant, as it indicates that Galen conceives of a broader theory of sound production, of which the interaction between cartilage and air represents merely the specific application of that general theory within living beings. Cartilages are a proper material since they have the right degree of hardness to maintain the proportionality: if they were too soft, they would yield to the air. If they were too hard, they would immediately overcome the air, and it would not resist for a short time (ἐπ᾿ ὀλίγον ἀνθίστασθαι τὸν ἀέρα). 
	The cartilages of the trachea provide rigidity and the proper material that allows air to resist for a short time. However, as will be shown next, while the material of the trachea is sufficient for phonation, there is not enough proportionality since the air is not (yet) at a sufficient force (ῥώμη). Therefore, Galen describes the contribution of the trachea as “preprocessing” (προρρυθμίζει) and preliminary preparation (προπαρασκευάζω) of the voice for the larynx. In On the Voice, it is explained that the trachea functions as a wide passage (via ampla) for the air (i.e., ἐκφύσησις) in which the air maintains its speed until reaching the larynx, where its speed increases because of the narrowing of the passage. Before moving on to the larynx, it behoves us to keep in mind Galen’s choice of the verb προρρυθμίζω. The verb is a compound of the verb ῥυθμίζω (lit. to bring something into measure/to shape) with the prefix προ-, denoting a preliminary action. The verb appears without the prefix in a passage from PHP, describing the larynx as the place where voice emerges: 
οὐ μὴν ἤδη γέ πω τὸ κάτωθεν ἀναφερόμενον πνεῦμα πρὶν ὑπὸ τοῦ λάρυγγος ῥυθμισθῆναι φωνῆς ἔχει ἰδέαν, ἀλλ’ ὡς εἴρηται πρόσθεν, ἐκφυσήσεως μόνης, ὡς ἀπεδείξαμεν ἐν τῇ περὶ φωνῆς πραγματείᾳ. πληττομένη γὰρ ὑπὸ τῶν κατὰ τὸν λάρυγγα χόνδρων οἷον ὑπὸ πλήκτρων τινῶν ἡ ἐκφύσησις αὕτη φωνὴ γίνεται. σύγκειται δὲ ὁ λάρυγξ ἐκ τριῶν χόνδρων ὑπὸ μυῶν πολλῶν κινουμένων. ὅστις δὲ ὁ τρόπος ἐστὶ τῆς τῶν χόνδρων συστάσεως ἢ τῆς τῶν μυῶν κινήσεως, ἐμοὶ μὲν ἐν ταῖς περὶ τούτων πραγματείαις οἰκείαις ἀκριβῶς εἴρηται· τοῖς δὲ ἀπὸ τῆς θέσεως μόνης ἐπιχειροῦσιν οὐδὲν τῶν τοιούτων γιγνώσκεται.
And indeed, the breath carried upwards from below does not have the form of voice before it has been shaped by the larynx, but as it was said before, it is only ἐκφύσησις, as I have demonstrated in my treatise On Voice. For the ἐκφύσησις, which is struck by the cartilages of the larynx as if it were struck by some plectrums, becomes this voice. The larynx is composed of three cartilages, which are moved by many muscles. And whichever is the manner of the consistency of these cartilages or the movement of the muscles, I gave an account in my treatises proper exactly regarding these matters: and any of such things is not known to those arguing from position alone. 
Gal. PHP. 2.4.26-28, 122,1-10 De Lacy = 5.232-233 Kühn
This passage is part of Galen’s argument against those who argue that voice is sent out from the heart, as part of their theory of the heart as the seat of the soul. Those who support that argument explain that the location of the heart, at approximately the middle of the body, is beneficial “for the sake of a just distribution of powers which it sends forth” (δικαίας ἕνεκα νομῆς ὧν ἐπιπέμπει δυνάμεων). These people use that proximity argument to describe the heart as the supplier of voice: the heart sends forth voice, already furnished, through the trachea (and the larynx), and it is plausible since the trachea is situated closely to the heart. Against that, Galen says in the passage above, that it is not possible that a furnished voice will be carried in the trachea since what travels in the trachea is not yet voice but ἐκφύσησις. Integrating the evidence from UP and On the Voice, the ἐκφύσησις is just not yet at the right amount of speed or force (ῥώμη) to become voice, with it being struck by the cartilaginous part of the trachea.  Galen has an established conception in his mind: air with an inevitable muscular contraction creates forceful exhalation – ἐκφύσησις. The ἐκφύσησις is the appropriate material (ὕλη τις οἰκεία) for voice, and voice is the proper material for speech. Galen needs to retain this sequence, which marks the focus on the emergence of voice in the larynx – the place where ἐκφύσησις undergoes a transformation into voice.
	In the larynx, the ἐκφύσησις is carried to a certain aperture, the glōttis. In this space, it is struck by the cartilages, in what is compared to something which is being struck by plectra. The analogy is partial: the cartilages are compared to plectrums, yet the ἐκφύσησις stays the same and is not likened to a string. Those plectra–cartilages are moved by the special twelve muscles of the larynx and controlled by the recurrent laryngeal nerves and the superior laryngeal nerve. As explained, before getting struck by the cartilages of the larynx, the ἐκφύσησις should gain more speed so that the proportion between his force and the cartilage will be sufficient to produce voice. To increase the speed of the ἐκφύσησις, nature has contrived inside the larynx the glōttis: a special body part that can narrow the passage in the larynx, which increases the speed of the ἐκφύσησις that travels through it. 
De Usu Partium 7.13 – the role of the glōttis
In UP.7.13, Galen presents a teleological account of human anatomy and physiology, arguing that every part of the body is purposefully designed by nature to fulfil a specific function. He combines detailed anatomical description with philosophical reasoning to demonstrate that bodily structures are optimally arranged for the preservation and flourishing of life. Compared with PHP, Galen’s writing is more focused on anatomical description that serves philosophical reasoning, rather than vice versa. As seen earlier in this work, the seventh book of the treatise is particularly significant for understanding Galen’s theory of voice production, as it provides detailed information on the organs involved in breathing and phonation. Galen begins by examining the lungs, focusing on their texture, shape, and connection to the heart, before moving on to the trachea and the mechanisms that regulate air movement. Then, his account proceeds into a richly detailed description of the larynx. Galen not only describes its cartilaginous structure and muscular attachments but also reflects on the harmony of its design, emphasising how each component works in coordinated interaction with others. Throughout the description, he reinforces the idea that such structures are not merely anatomically necessary but are functionally and intelligently arranged, offering, in his view, unmistakable evidence of nature’s providential craftsmanship. 
In Chapter 13 of the book, the glōttis, a structure located inside the larynx, is introduced. As its anatomy was previously discussed in the chapter, this discussion will build on that analysis, combining it with a physiological analysis in the same manner as has been practised so far in this work. The current debate aims to gain a clear understanding of the physiological mechanism Galen attributes to the glōttis: a dynamic regulator of air, designed to narrow the passage inside the larynx and increase the speed of the approaching ἐκφύσησις while withstanding high-pressure levels of air, or, to use a less anachronistic term, high “force” (ῥώμη) levels of air. First, the glōttis should be held tightly from the outside, and not “fall back”, as Galen describes in the following passage: 
εἰ γὰρ ἀναπεπταμένη τελέως εἴη σύμπασα, κεχαλασμένων μὲν τῶν πρώτων δυοῖν χόνδρων καὶ διεστώτων ἀπ’ ἀλλήλων, ἀνεῳγμένου δὲ τοῦ τρίτου, μή ποτ’ ἂν δύνασθαι γενέσθαι φωνήν· ἀλλ’ εἰ μὲν ἀτρέμα τὸ πνεῦμα ἔξω φέροιτο, τὴν χωρὶς ψόφου συντελουμένην ἐκπνοήν· εἰ δ’ ἀθρόως τε καὶ σφοδρῶς, τὸ καλούμενον στενάζειν γιγνόμενον. ἵνα δὲ φωνήσῃ τὸ ζῶον, δεῖσθαι πάντως καὶ τῆς κάτωθεν φορᾶς ἀθροωτέρας, δεῖσθαι δ’ οὐδὲν ἧττον ταύτης καὶ τῆς κατὰ τὸν λάρυγγα διεξόδου στενωτέρας, καὶ οὐχ ἁπλῶς γε στενωτέρας, ἀλλὰ κατὰ βραχὺ μὲν ἐξ εὐρέος εἰς στενὸν ἀγομένης, κατὰ βραχὺ δ’ ἐκ τοῦ στενοῦ πάλιν εὐρυνομένης. ὅπερ ἀκριβῶς ἐργάζεται τουτὶ τὸ σῶμα τὸ προκείμενον ἐν τῷ λόγῳ νῦν, ὃ δὴ γλωττίδα τε καὶ γλῶσσαν ὀνομάζω λάρυγγος.
For if only the whole passage would entirely fall back, while the first two cartilages (thyroid and cricoid) are loosed and separated from each other, and The third (arytenoid) lied open, lest ever it would be possible to produce voice: but if the air would be carried with no motion outside, a soundless expiration will be achieved: but if the air would be carried at once and vehemently, what is called “to sigh” will be produced. In order that the animal would phonate, it requires a sudden carrying (of pneuma) from all of the parts below, and it requires no less of that and the passage in the larynx to be narrow and not simply narrow, but it is led gradually from the wide to the narrow part, and gradually it is widens again back from the narrow part. That is exactly how that body in our current discussion works, which indeed I name glōttis and the tongue of the larynx.
Gal. UP. 7.13, 408-409,18-7 Helmreich = 3.561-562 Kühn
Galen introduces a hypothetical situation in which the narrowing passage “would entirely fall back” (ἀναπεπταμένη τελέως εἴη σύμπασα): under such conditions, the thyroid and cricoid cartilages would be loose, and the arytenoid cartilage would be laid open, keeping the passage wide. Such a case happens if the thyroarytenoid muscles are not functioning, i.e., not confining the arytenoid cartilage and closing the passage. In such a case, the glōttis does not narrow significantly. There are two possible outcomes: ἐκπνοή, which is explicitly mentioned by name, and “noisy ἐκφύσησις”, described here by: “what is called ‘to sigh’” (τὸ καλούμενον στενάζειν γιγνόμενον). Then, Galen integrates the requirement for the gradual narrowing of the glōttis along with the “sudden carrying” (φορᾶς ἀθροωτέρας) of the air, i.e., ἐκφύσησις. Per Galen, the combination of the two is necessary so that the animal can phonate:
ἀντέχουσι γὰρ οὗτοι βιαίως ὠθουμένῳ τῷ πνεύματι τὸν ἀρυταινοειδῆ κλείοντες χόνδρον, εἰς ὅπερ ἔργον οὐ σμικρὰ συντελεῖ τῆς προειρημένης γλωττίδος ἡ φύσις. εἰς ταὐτὸν γὰρ αὐτῆς ἔρχεται τὰ μόρια, τό τ’ ἐκ τῶν ἀριστερῶν καὶ τὸ τῶν δεξιῶν, ὡς συμπεσεῖν ἀλλήλοις ἀκριβῶς καὶ κλείεσθαι τὸν πόρον.
For those [muscles] confining the arytenoid cartilage forcibly resist against the pushed air, the nature of the aforesaid glōttis significantly contributes to this function. For the parts of the glōttis, both the left and the right, come to it, so that they fall together to each other and accurately close the passage. 
Gal. UP. 7. 13. 409. 15-21 Helmreich = 3. 563 Kühn
	This passage describes the importance of the arytenoid cartilage (with the muscles confining it) in the narrowing of the passage of the larynx. The thyroarytenoid muscles, connected to the arytenoid cartilage, are facing a large amount of air pushed against them. It is not entirely clear from only reading the text: the thyroarytenoid muscles are located below the vestibular folds (“parts of the glōttis”). Therefore, when the parts of the glōttis are shut and the passage is narrowed, most of the air is held inside the glottis and presses much on the thyroarytenoid muscles, which must stay closed to keep the narrowing of the passage. Galen solves this problem by saying that in the “larger-voiced” animals (μεγαλοφώνοις), there is a small portion of the opening that is always open, and that there is in all animals a mechanism of two cavities below the parts of the glōttis, ready to store in them some of the air to reduce the force of the air which presses the shut glōttis. The mechanism works in such a way that when the glottis becomes accurately narrow (ἀκριβῶς δὲ στενοῦσθαι), the ἐκφύσησις, instead of going extremely forcefully and opening the narrowed passage by force, only the necessary mass of it goes out (ἀποχεῖσθαι μὲν δήπου τὸν ὄγκον ἀναγκαῖον), and the rest goes sideways to the cavities. The parts of the glōttis act like a valve that opens only when needed: In respiration, the passage should not be excessively narrow, as it is preferable to permit the free movement of air in and out. The structure of these valves, i.e., the parts of the glōttis, and the cavities beneath them, makes the air spin inside in what Galen calls “some kind of a whirlpool experience” (οἷον ἴλιγγόν τινα παθὸν). This is an important detail: the whirlpool experience that Galen describes can be interpreted as the way in which the structure serves its purpose (χρεία): the whirlpool experience may indicate that Galen emphasises that the air remains within the cavities. It does not apply pressure to them, as they are designed to maintain continuous motion. However, it is an interpretation and not stated in the text. There is no definitive way to prove that this is what Galen meant. Galen’s theory, complex as it would be, does not follow observation in this case. Although he observed the anatomical structure in many specimens and performed vivisection on a phonating specimen, he does not claim to have followed the physiological mechanism with his own eyes, nor does he guide his reader to attempt to find such an observation. Galen’s theory on this physiological mechanism, which is part of his theory of phonation, is speculative. This raises the question of whether he observed behaviour in his demonstrations on live animals that led him to consider this theory, or whether it is a logical leap he draws from observations he could confirm. Either way, the answer is absent from the text. 
Conclusions
This chapter has clarified the physiological framework through which Galen explains voice production. Beginning with the nature of ἐκφύσησις, the analysis demonstrated that voice cannot arise from regular exhalation but requires a distinct, intensive activity produced by coordinated thoracic musculature. ἐκφύσησις emerges as both suitable material for voice (ὕλη τις οἰκεία φωνῆς) as well as an activity (ἐνέργεια); It is a material open to modification and distinguished by a particular intensity. Yet, taken on its own, it yields only “noisy exhalation”: a sigh. As shown, when moving in the trachea, although the trachea has the proper cartilaginous material to strike ἐκφύσησις and produce voice, the force (ῥώμη) of the ἐκφύσησις is not strong enough to create proportional impact and generate voice.  A further stage of shaping is required before voice can emerge. The second part of the chapter examined the mechanisms that perform this shaping. Phonation results from a systematic interaction between ἐκφύσησις and the structures of the larynx: cartilages, muscles, nerves, and, crucially, the glōttis. Galen’s emphasis on proportionality between the striker (cartilage) and the force of the being struck (air) requires a well-coordinated structure capable of providing this proportionality for both sides. The cartilages of the larynx possess the appropriate material properties, yet the force of ἐκφύσησις must be augmented before impact. The glōttis fulfils precisely this role. By providing a narrowing passage that elevates the force of the air while withstanding the pressure of excess air, the glōttis ensures the proper conditions for the cartilaginous strike that produces voice. 
Across this analysis, a consistent teleological logic becomes apparent. As in the anatomical chapter, the physiology of phonation reveals the features that Rebecca Flemming identifies as characteristic of Galen’s demiurgic Nature (φύσις): having both forethought (πρόνοια) and skill (τέχνη), nature can achieve purposeful arrangement and make the design of each part to secure the best possible physiological outcome when it functions. The larynx exemplifies this mode of reasoning. Its shape, composition (including the glōttis and its mechanism), placement, and coordinated movements with its muscles and nerves are all evidence of nature’s provident planning. Similarly, the trachea’s preparatory function and the hardness of its cartilaginous part follow the same teleological logic. The chapter has thus shown that teleology in Galen is not an ornament added to anatomy, but an epistemic principle guiding the unfolding of physiological processes – precisely the function Flemming identifies: the demiurge provides the intelligible order that makes physiological explanation possible. 
The analysis of the physiological mechanisms in this chapter has also demonstrated the value of the methodological framework established in the previous chapter regarding the role of analogy in Galenic explanation. Understanding how Galen utilises controlled, functionally oriented analogies, particularly those drawn from music, clarifies why the comparison between cartilages and plectra, as well as the mouthpiece of the aulos and the glōttis, is not merely illustrative but central to reconstructing the causal sequence of phonation. The epistemic framework established earlier thus enables a more precise reading of how impact, resistance, and proportionality operate in the larynx. In sum, this chapter has argued that Galen conceives phonation not as a spontaneous by-product of exhalation, but as the result of a meticulously arranged sequence of events in which ἐκφύσησις is prepared, accelerated, and ultimately struck into voice. This physiologically grounded and teleologically immersed model reinforces the broader epistemic and anatomical framework established in the previous chapter, laying the conceptual groundwork for the thematic conclusions of this work as a whole. 



Conclusions
This study has examined scattered anatomical and physiological material relating to phonation across Galen’s corpus and has reconstructed a coherent model of phonation from it. What emerges is a unified conception of voice production anchored in Galen’s characteristic combination of anatomical investigation, physiological inquiry, and teleological explanation. Although Galen nowhere offers a single, continuous exposition of the anatomy and physiology of phonation, the present work has demonstrated that he nevertheless possessed an integrated theoretical framework in which carefully described structures act in coordination to produce voice. The reconstruction attempted here has therefore served a dual function: to clarify Galen’s doctrine of phonation itself, and to illuminate more broadly the methods by which anatomical observations allow him to construct physiological knowledge. Across both chapters, several central findings have surfaced. The first concerns the extent and precision of Galen’s anatomical account, by gathering material from AA, UP, De Diss. Musc., and other treatises, it became possible to reconstruct the fixed set of organs Galen understood as participating in phonation. The analysis revealed that Galen’s descriptions display not only high technical skill but also a sustained conceptual organisation: the repeated emphasis on paired structures, the progression between different layers of the body from covering to deep structures, and the detailed relationships between cartilages, muscles, nerves, and membranous tissues all demonstrate a systematic anatomical vision. Galen’s discovery and exposition of the RLN, his attention to subtle muscles often overlooked or destroyed by his predecessors, and his description of unique structures such as the glōttis underscore both his originality and his commitment to conceptual perfection. 
The second broad finding concerns the teleological structuring of Galen’s anatomical thought. As the first chapter showed, Galen’s account is not a descriptive inventory but a purposive analysis: each body part is explained through its material composition, spatial arrangement, and intended function. This teleological mode of explanation, grounded in the conception that nature is a craftsman possessing both skill (τέχνη) and forethought (πρόνοια), enables Galen to integrate his anatomical observations into a meaningful system. The hyoid bone and its muscular connections, the cartilaginous rings of the trachea, the composition of the laryngeal cartilages, the continuity of mucous membranes, and the distinct design of the glōttis all become intelligible when viewed as components crafted to serve a specific end. The anatomical chapter of this work, therefore, revealed that for Galen, anatomical structures are never random but consistently exemplify nature’s purposive design. This insight becomes decisive at the physiological level. The second chapter examined the physiological mechanisms that transform ἐκφύσησις into voice, and it is here that the necessity of anatomical reconstruction became apparent. Galen conceives phonation as a dynamic sequence of events rather than a static property of a single organ, in contrast to the conception of some of his adversaries. The analysis showed that ἐκφύσησις is not merely intensified breath, but a forceful, muscularly generated activity (ἐνέργεια) that is the “suitable matter” (ὕλη οἰκεία) of voice. Yet, as demonstrated, ἐκφύσησις alone cannot yield voice: in its unmodified form, it produces only soundless exhalation or a “noisy” (ψοφώδης) sigh. Phonation requires more than suitable matter; it requires the actions of the appropriate structures and the proper structural conditions. This leads to the central physiological insight of this study: voice arises only when ἐκφύσησις, having been accelerated through the narrowing of the glōttis, meets the proportional resistance of the laryngeal cartilages. The glōttis emerges as the indispensable organ of transformation. Its paired structure of two parts and two ventricles allows it to narrow to the precise degree and withstand the high pressure necessary to increase the force (ῥώμη) of ἐκφύσησις. The resulting air current, now sufficiently accelerated, is then struck by the cartilages, functioning analogically like plectra. Crucially, the analysis demonstrated why the cartilaginous structure of the trachea cannot produce voice, though it possesses the right “striking” material: the proportionality between the striker and the struck (ἐκφύσησις) is inadequate until the modification in the glōttis. Galen thus conceptualises phonation as a two-stage shaping, a conception reinforced with his careful choice of vocabulary: preliminary preparation in the trachea (προρρυθμίζει) and final shaping in the larynx (ῥυθμισθῆναι). To this rhetorical nuance, Galen’s exceptional use of analogical explanation tools also contributes. Galen’s analogical reasoning, especially the musical analogies, clarifies how he guides his reader from the familiar and clearly visible to the foreign and hidden deep in the body. The comparison of the glōttis to the mouthpiece of an aulos, and of laryngeal cartilages to plectra, emerged not only as a rhetorical embellishment but as functional explanatory tools. These analogies operate within limits clearly set by Galen: they assist conceptualisation without distorting or replacing anatomical reality. For Galen, important as these tools may be, they are secondary to the experience of learning by observing the body during dissections. The usage of analogies by Galen in the case of phonation aligns well with the broader pattern in Greek scientific writing outlined by Lloyd, von Staden, and Ferella, but is distinctive in Galen’s insistence on pairing analogy with the practice of dissection. Analogy bridges the hidden interior of the larynx to the intelligible mechanism required for physiological explanation, which is apparent and accessible in musical instruments. Taken together, these findings reveal a comprehensive pattern: Galen’s theory of phonation is a paradigmatic instance of functional anatomy. Anatomical structures acquire significance through their place within physiological processes, and physiological processes are intelligible only when grounded in the material and spatial reality of anatomical parts. The relation between structure and function, neither reducible to the other but always intertwined, enables Galen to articulate a model of phonation that is both empirically and philosophically well grounded. 
Finally, this study has demonstrated that Galen’s reconstruction of phonation constitutes an epistemological turning point in ancient thought on voice. Earlier accounts tended to localise voice production in single structures (e.g., the heart, the tongue, or the trachea), lacking both anatomical precision and physiological integration. In contrast, Galen’s model conceives voice as the outcome of coordinated actions among multiple organs: bones, cartilages, muscles, nerves, membranes, and cavities. Voice is not the property of a particular bodily location but rather the product of a system. In this respect, Galen’s model anticipates later understandings of physiological processes. The conclusions of this thesis thus point beyond phonation alone. Galen’s account of voice production exemplifies the broader principles of his medical theory: the indispensability of anatomy to physiology, the teleological structure of bodily organisation, the role of controlled analogy in articulating hidden processes, and the systematic integration of matter, movement, and purpose. Phonation becomes a case study in how Galen thinks about the body as a whole: a purposively designed system in which every part contributes to an intelligible functional order. In reconstructing Galen’s doctrine of phonation, this study has therefore illuminated not only the nature of voice in Galenic medicine but also the methods by which Galen transforms anatomical knowledge into physiological explanation. Reconstructing the anatomical and physiological foundations of Galen’s theory of phonation validates this study as a platform upon which further investigation on the nature of voice may be built. By clarifying the structures and mechanisms that collectively produce voice, it becomes possible to explore various discussions at the intersection of physiology, physics, and metaphysics in Galen’s thought: whether voice is a transformation of pneuma, how it relates to the psychic faculties, and how its status as a meaningful sound (σημαντικὸς ψόφος) positions it within Galen’s broader ontology of different auditory phenomena. Likewise, the present reconstruction provides indispensable groundwork for examining aspects of voice that are deliberately outside the scope of this work. These include the role of the brain and neural faculties in initiating vocal activity, the articulation of the voice into speech through additional body parts such as the tongue, palate, and teeth, and the modulation of the voice in pitch, volume, and timbre. Each of these topics requires the secure anatomical and physiological basis established in this work. In this way, the study offers both a complete account of Galen’s model of phonation and a necessary point of departure for future enquiries into the multifaceted phenomenon of voice in Galenic medicine and philosophy.
